# QA

Q1. Who is the respondent and what is the administrative proceeding file number?
Expected: Keurig Dr Pepper Inc.; Administrative Proceeding File No. 3-22100 (Securities Exchange Act Release No. 100983).

Q2. On what date was the order issued?
Expected: September 10, 2024.

Q3. What statements are at the center of the order?
Expected: Statements in Keurig's annual reports (Forms 10-K) for fiscal years 2019 and 2020 that its testing with municipal recycling facilities "validate[d] that [pods] can be effectively recycled."

Q4. What did the SEC find was wrong with those statements?
Expected: They were incomplete and therefore inaccurate because they did not disclose the negative feedback Keurig received from two large recycling companies about the commercial feasibility of curbside recycling of the pods.

Q5. What securities-law provisions did Keurig violate?
Expected: Section 13(a) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Rule 13a-1 thereunder (the requirement to file complete and accurate annual reports).

Q6. What civil penalty did Keurig agree to pay, and within what timeframe?
Expected: $1,500,000, within 14 days of the entry of the order.

Q7. When did Keurig begin testing its pods at recycling facilities, and how did the tests track the pods?
Expected: Around 2016; the tests used radio frequency identification (RFID) readers to follow pods tagged with a tracking chip through the recycling process.

Q8. What did the testing actually demonstrate about the pods?
Expected: That pods typically could be successfully sorted from other materials at an early stage of the recycling process.

Q9. What did one of the recycling companies write to Keurig?
Expected: That "there simply is not a sufficient benefit for small format materials, and/or hard-to-recycle materials – including K-Cup pods – to make the financial case for inclusion as part of curbside recycling programs."

Q10. How significant were the two recycling companies that gave negative feedback?
Expected: They are among the nation's largest recycling companies, operating more than one-third of recycling facilities in the United States.

Q11. What 2014 sustainability goal did Keurig Green Mountain announce?
Expected: To make 100% of the pods it manufactured recyclable by 2020.

Q12. What material did Keurig switch its pods to, and by when?
Expected: Polypropylene number 5 plastic (PP5); all pods manufactured for sale in the U.S. and Canada were made of PP5 by the end of 2020.

Q13. What financial commitment did Keurig reference in its FY2020 10-K regarding polypropylene recycling?
Expected: A $10 million commitment toward the advancement of polypropylene recycling through the Polypropylene Recycling Coalition (led by The Recycling Partnership).

Q14. What happened to the recyclability-testing discussion in later filings?
Expected: Beginning with the Form 10-K for the year ended December 31, 2021 (filed February 24, 2022), Keurig's 10-Ks did not contain any discussion of its recyclability testing.

Q15. Did Keurig admit the SEC's findings?
Expected: No; Keurig submitted an Offer of Settlement and consented to the order without admitting or denying the findings, except as to the Commission's jurisdiction.
