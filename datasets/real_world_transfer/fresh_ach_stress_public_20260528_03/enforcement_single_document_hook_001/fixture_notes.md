# fixture_notes — enforcement_single_document_hook_001

## Why this source was chosen
A short, self-contained SEC settled cease-and-desist order whose entire violation theory turns on one omitted fact. It is an ideal "single document hook" high-sensitivity case: the order quotes the offending statement, recites the positive test results, and then pins the violation on a single omitted piece of adverse information. It is fresh public-record material not used in any prior batch.

## Why the sensitivity target is high
The order's theory is a half-truth: the affirmative statement that testing "validate[d] that [pods] can be effectively recycled" became "incomplete and therefore inaccurate" only because Keurig omitted the negative feedback from two of the nation's largest recyclers. That omitted feedback (e1) is the lone hinge. Remove it and the statement is an accurate description of genuinely positive sorting tests (e4), so the violation theory has nothing to stand on. One row carries the whole read — the defining property of a high-sensitivity fixture.

## Tempting wrong hypothesis
Outright falsity (h2): a reader may assume the SEC found the recyclability claim simply false. The order forecloses this — the tests "reflected that pods typically could be successfully sorted from other materials" (e4). The violation is incompleteness, not falsity.

## Off-axis true hypothesis
Sustainability framing (h3): the 2014 100%-recyclable-by-2020 goal, the PP5 conversion, and the $10 million Polypropylene Recycling Coalition commitment (e6) are all true and prominent in the document, but the order does not locate the violation there. This is the batch's required off-axis-true hypothesis: real background, wrong answer to the ACH question.

## Direct / partial / off-axis rows
- Direct to h1 (omission/half-truth): e1 (pivotal), e3 (incompleteness conclusion), e5 (weight of omitted source), e7 (completeness requirement of the charged rule).
- Identifies the statement (relevant to all): e2.
- Direct knock-down of h2 (falsity): e4 (positive sorting tests).
- Off-axis to h3: e6.
- Context/materiality (partial to h1): e8.

## Double-edged rows
- e8 (environmental concerns a significant purchase factor; pods a significant share of sales) cuts two ways: the order uses it to show the omission's significance (supports h1), but a reader could over-read the consumer-purchasing framing toward the sustainability hypothesis (h3).
- e2 is, by itself, an accurate statement; it only becomes inculpatory when paired with e1, which is precisely why e1 is pivotal rather than e2.

## Extraction quirks
- The order quotes the 10-K language with bracketed substitutions ("[pods]") exactly as the SEC rendered it; the bracket text is preserved in source.md and should be matched as-is.
- Two filing dates appear for the two 10-Ks (Feb 27, 2020 for FY2019; Feb 25, 2021 for FY2020) plus a later FY2021 filing date (Feb 24, 2022); do not conflate the fiscal year with the filing year.
- Payment-mechanics and penalty-offset paragraphs are administrative boilerplate and are summarized rather than reproduced; they carry no facts load-bearing for the ACH or QA.
