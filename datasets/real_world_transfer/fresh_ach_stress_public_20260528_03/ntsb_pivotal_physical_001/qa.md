# QA

Q1. What was the NTSB's determined probable cause of the accident?
Expected: The improper maintenance of the carburetor, which resulted in a stuck float and a partial loss of engine power during takeoff.

Q2. What is the accident number and date?
Expected: WPR17LA096; May 2, 2017.

Q3. What aircraft make and model was involved, and what engine did it have?
Expected: A Piper PA 32-260 (1966), powered by a Lycoming O-540-E4B5 rated at 260 horsepower.

Q4. What were the injuries?
Expected: The private pilot was not injured; the passenger received minor injuries (1 Minor, 1 None).

Q5. What did the witness observe during takeoff?
Expected: Bursts of visible "exhaust" from the airplane — an abnormal exhaust trail that drew his attention.

Q6. What did examination of the spark plugs show?
Expected: Several plug electrodes were coated in dark grey/black soot corresponding to "carbon fouled," indicative of an excessively rich fuel-to-air mixture.

Q7. What specific carburetor float discrepancy was found?
Expected: The float-to-bowl clearance was out of tolerance; rub marks on the bowl walls indicated float contact; and the float was slightly twisted (one side resting 1/16th inch above the other at its tip).

Q8. Which service bulletins were referenced, and was compliance documented?
Expected: SB-4 Rev. B (Volare, float clearance), SB-10 Rev B (Marvel-Schebler, unauthorized thread sealant), and MSA-3 rev. 1 (Precision Airmotive, 10-year carburetor overhaul). No logbook entry showed SB-4 compliance, and white thread sealant (prohibited by SB-10) was found.

Q9. What did the meteorological data indicate about carburetor icing?
Expected: The FAA SAIB CE-09-35 icing probability chart indicated a probability of serious icing at cruise power for the reported temperature (15°C) and dew point (9°C).

Q10. Why did the report consider carburetor icing unlikely despite the conducive conditions?
Expected: The airplane began the takeoff roll almost immediately after an engine runup that included applying carburetor heat, so ice was unlikely to have formed and persisted.

Q11. What did fuel testing and the fuel supply investigation find?
Expected: Fuel in the carburetor bowl was clean, bright, light-blue, and tested negative for water; tanks were fueled ~30 minutes prior; sump checks were normal; and the FBO found no water in storage and no issues from two other aircraft fueled afterward.

Q12. What happened during the post-accident engine test run?
Expected: After two cranking attempts the engine started and ran smoothly for about 4 minutes from 700 to 2,500 rpm; each magneto showed a 100 rpm drop at 1,500 rpm; and the black soot on the plugs was gone afterward, replaced by dark grey coloration.

Q13. Why did the pilot accept an intersection departure, and what did the report say about that decision?
Expected: A Boeing 787 was performing tests at the arrival end of runway 16R, so the pilot accepted an intersection departure with about 5,600 ft remaining; the report noted that using the full runway length would likely have left enough runway to land safely after the power loss.

Q14. Did the NTSB travel to the scene?
Expected: Yes — the report states the NTSB traveled to the scene of this accident.

Q15. How much fuel was added before the flight and what type?
Expected: 41.2 gallons of 100 octane low-lead aviation gasoline, about 30 minutes before the accident.
