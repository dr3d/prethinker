# OSHRC Decision and Order — Secretary of Labor v. RK Hydro-Vac, Inc., Docket No. 03-1583

> Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission, Administrative Law Judge Nancy J. Spies. Public record (US Government work). Body text verbatim/near-verbatim from the Decision and Order; bracketed headings added as citation coordinates; transcript/exhibit citations (Tr., Exh.) preserved as in the original.

[CAPTION]
Secretary of Labor, Complainant, v. RK Hydro-Vac, Inc., Respondent. OSHRC Docket No. 03-1583. For Complainant: Mary Beth Zamer Bernui, Esq., Office of the Solicitor, U.S. Department of Labor. For Respondent: Jay St. Clair, Esq., Bradley, Arant, Rose & White, LLP. Before: Administrative Law Judge Nancy J. Spies.

[SUMMARY]
R. K. Hydro-Vac, Inc. (RKH) is a specialty contractor in the commercial roofing industry. As a subcontractor, it vacuums gravel off roofs for reroofing projects undertaken by a general contractor. On July 23, 2003, OSHA compliance officer Michelle Sotak conducted an inspection of an RKH worksite in Birmingham, Alabama. As a result, the Secretary issued a one-item citation to RKH on August 1, 2003, alleging that RKH committed a serious violation of § 1926.501(b)(1) for failing to require an employee working at the edge of a 30-foot building to use fall protection. This case was designated as an E-Z proceeding. A hearing was held November 13, 2003. RKH asserts the affirmative defense of unpreventable employee misconduct. For the reasons discussed below, RKH's employee misconduct defense is rejected and the citation is affirmed, with a substantial reduction in the Secretary's proposed penalty.

[BACKGROUND]
On July 23, 2003, compliance officer Michelle Sotak was driving on Valleydale Road in Birmingham, Alabama, when she observed a man on the roof of a building who did not appear to be using fall protection. She pulled over and took pictures of the man leaning over the edge of the building's roof (Exhs. C-1, C-2, C-3; Tr. 13-14). A string of flags used as an awareness barrier had been placed 6 feet from the edge of the roof; it had been taken down where the employee was located (Exh. C-2; Tr. 19). After the employee noticed Sotak taking pictures, he stepped away from the edge and replaced the awareness barrier (Exh. C-4; Tr. 20).

Sotak met with the general contractor's site manager, Mr. Faircloth, who stated that the man on the roof was an employee of subcontractor RKH. The roof was approximately 30 feet high (Tr. 21). Sotak then met Mike Petrey and the man who had been on the roof, Fernando Acateca, both RKH employees. When asked why Acateca had not been using fall protection, both stated that Acateca had been tied off while on the roof prior to their lunch break. Acateca told Sotak "that he forgot to put [his safety harness] back on when he went up to the roof after lunch" (Tr. 16). RKH had a safety harness available on site for Acateca and he put it on in Sotak's presence (Tr. 17). RKH is based in Charlotte, North Carolina, with satellite offices in Florida and Georgia, has been in business about 20 years, and employs approximately 32 employees who work in two-man crews.

[THE CITATION — ELEMENTS]
To establish a violation, the Secretary has the burden of proving: (a) the applicability of the cited standard, (b) the employer's noncompliance, (c) employee access to the violative conditions, and (d) the employer's actual or constructive knowledge of the violation (i.e., the employer either knew or, with the exercise of reasonable diligence could have known, of the violative conditions). The Secretary alleges a serious violation of § 1926.501(b)(1), which requires each employee on a surface with an unprotected side or edge 6 feet or more above a lower level to be protected from falling by guardrail systems, safety net systems, or personal fall arrest systems.

It is undisputed that § 1926.501(b)(1) applies. RKH concedes that Acateca was not tied off and was not using any other form of fall protection, in violation of the standard. Exhibits C-1, C-2, and C-3 show that Acateca was leaning over the immediate edge of the building, exposed to a 30-foot fall. The only element of the Secretary's case at issue is whether RKH had actual or constructive knowledge of the violation.

[ACTUAL KNOWLEDGE]
At the hearing, the Secretary proceeded on the theory that Petrey was Acateca's supervisor and that his knowledge could be imputed to RKH. The testimony of RKH witnesses Michael Corigliano (RKH's part-time safety director and CDL driving trainer) and owner Richard Girouard undercut that position: the two crew members were equal in safety training and job responsibility; one crew member had no supervisory authority over the other, no authority to hire, fire, or order people around, and the CDL designation made one the lead only while driving the trucks. In her post-hearing brief, the Secretary does not argue that Petrey was Acateca's supervisor and does not argue that RKH had actual knowledge; rather she contends RKH could have known with the exercise of reasonable diligence (constructive knowledge). The record does not establish that RKH had actual knowledge of Acateca's failure to tie off.

[CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE — WORK RULES]
An employer is assumed to have constructive knowledge of a violative condition if the condition could have been discovered with reasonable diligence. RKH has an extensive written safety program, given to all employees (Exh. R-1). Its fall-protection work rules are consistent with § 1926.501(b)(1). The work rules are determined to be adequate.

[CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE — TRAINING]
The record establishes that RKH has an exemplary training program. New employees receive a safety manual and safety bag and attend mandatory training; initial safety training lasts 11 hours. Twice a year all employees attend a mandatory 3-day training seminar; both Petrey and Acateca attended these seminars (Exhs. R-2, R-3). RKH's safety training program is determined to be more than adequate.

[CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE — SUPERVISION]
The two-man crews work without on-site supervision. RKH contends it provides supervision through random site visits by its safety director, its owner, and its insurance carrier. Corigliano conceded his on-site visits are limited to North Carolina and that he would be unable to visit a worksite in Alabama (Tr. 53). Girouard testified that RKH had approximately ten jobs in Alabama in the past year and that he had visited none of them (Tr. 74). There was no evidence the insurance carrier ever inspected an Alabama site. Based on the record, while RKH may adequately supervise its employees in North Carolina, it does not provide adequate supervision of its employees on its Alabama worksites. No one from RKH or its insurance carrier had been to Alabama in the past year. RKH employees on the Alabama worksites were aware that it was unlikely a surprise inspection would take place. This lack of supervisory oversight is evidence of failure to exercise reasonable diligence to discover violative conditions. Not only did Acateca violate RKH's work rule requiring him to tie off, he did so in the presence of Petrey, who failed to remind Acateca to tie off. RKH's failure to provide adequate supervision over its Alabama worksite establishes its constructive knowledge of the § 1926.501(b)(1) violation.

[CONSTRUCTIVE KNOWLEDGE — ENFORCEMENT POLICY]
RKH's constructive knowledge is also established by its lack of an enforcement policy. Despite being RKH's safety director, Corigliano testified that his safety training does not include any discussion of a disciplinary system, stating, "That's not my job" (Tr. 52), and he took no part in disciplining Petrey and Acateca after the inspection. Girouard testified that RKH does not have a graduated disciplinary scale and that enforcement is not part of the safety program (Tr. 75). Failure to implement an enforcement or disciplinary policy is evidence of lack of reasonable diligence.

[UNPREVENTABLE EMPLOYEE MISCONDUCT DEFENSE]
To establish the affirmative defense of employee misconduct, the employer must prove (1) it established work rules designed to prevent the violation, (2) it adequately communicated these rules, (3) it took steps to discover violations, and (4) it effectively enforced the rules when violations are discovered. The Review Commission has noted that the elements of proof for the employee misconduct defense overlap the proof establishing constructive knowledge. The constructive-knowledge analysis determined that RKH had an established work rule (element 1) and had adequately communicated it through training (element 2), but failed to take steps to discover the violation through adequate supervision (element 3) and failed to effectively enforce the rule (element 4). RKH's employee misconduct defense fails for the same reasons that constructive knowledge was held to be established.

[PENALTY DETERMINATION]
RKH employed approximately 32 employees and had no prior history with OSHA. The gravity of the violation is high — Acateca was leaning over the edge of a roof 30 feet above the ground without fall protection, and death would be the likely result of a fall. The Secretary proposed a penalty of $2,500.00. Despite the high gravity, a significant reduction is warranted due to RKH's history and good faith: RKH has an extensive written safety program, provides each employee with a complete safety bag and training, and had never been cited in its 20-year history. An appropriate penalty is determined to be $750.00.

[ORDER]
It is ORDERED that Item 1 of the citation, alleging a serious violation of § 1926.501(b)(1), is affirmed, and a penalty of $750.00 is assessed.
