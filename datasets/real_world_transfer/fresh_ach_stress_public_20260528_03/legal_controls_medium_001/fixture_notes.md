# fixture_notes — legal_controls_medium_001

## Why this source was chosen
A compact, fully adjudicated OSHRC Decision and Order in which responsibility for a fall-protection violation is genuinely contested between an institutional/control reading (the employer's safety system failed) and an individual/operator reading (a trained employee simply forgot). The decision narrows itself to a single legal element (knowledge), which keeps the ACH clean, and it is fresh public-record material not used in any prior batch. It fits the "institutional vs individual" pressure for a legal/regulatory order.

## Why the sensitivity target is medium
The winning reading — employer responsibility via constructive knowledge — does not rest on one row. It rests on two independent causal-bridge findings: inadequate supervision of the remote Alabama worksites (e4) and the absence of an enforcement/disciplinary policy (e5). Because the unpreventable-employee-misconduct defense requires all four of its elements and the employer already satisfied two of them strongly (adequate rules e2, exemplary training e3), the defense is genuinely close. Removing either e4 or e5 alone leaves one pillar of constructive knowledge standing and still defeats one misconduct element, so the winner survives but is materially weakened. Removing both e4 and e5 would flip the read to employee misconduct. That two-row-to-flip / one-row-to-weaken behavior is the medium definition.

## Tempting wrong hypothesis
Unpreventable employee misconduct (h2). It has real support in the document: the employer had adequate written rules and an exemplary training program, and the immediate cause was the employee's own admitted lapse (he forgot to re-tie off after lunch). A reader who stops at "good program + employee error" will wrongly exonerate the employer.

## Direct / partial / off-axis rows
- Direct to h1 (employer / constructive knowledge): e4 (supervision gap), e5 (enforcement gap), e8 (UEM fails for the same reasons).
- Direct to h2 (employee misconduct): e2 (adequate rules), e3 (exemplary training), e7 (employee's lapse).
- Direct knock-down of h3 (actual supervisor knowledge): e6.
- Framing row: e1 (only the knowledge element is contested).
- Off-axis: e9 (penalty reduced for good faith) — true and material to the fine, but it does not move responsibility off the employer; it is the intentional off-axis row for this batch.

## Double-edged rows
- e7 (employee forgot to re-tie off) is double-edged: on its face it supports the misconduct reading (h2), but the fact that it happened in a co-worker's presence and went undiscovered also feeds the employer-diligence analysis (h1).
- e2 and e3 (rules and training) support h2 yet are also the very findings the decision uses to show the employer met only two of the four misconduct elements, framing the failure on the other two.

## Extraction quirks
- The decision is an E-Z (expedited) proceeding and interleaves transcript citations (Tr.) and exhibit citations (Exh.); these are preserved in source.md and are not load-bearing for any QA answer.
- "Constructive knowledge" and the misconduct defense overlap by design in OSHRC law; the source states this explicitly, so the same two findings (e4, e5) appear in both the knowledge analysis and the defense analysis. The ACH should treat them as one shared pair of causal-bridge rows, not double-count them.
- Two coworkers (Acateca, Petrey) and several RKH figures (Corigliano, Girouard, Shafer) appear; only Acateca's exposure and the supervision/enforcement findings are load-bearing for the responsibility question.
