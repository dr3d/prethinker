# QA

Q1. Who are the respondents and what is the docket number?
Expected: Marriott International, Inc. and Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide, LLC; Docket No. C-4807 (FTC Matter/File No. 192 3022).

Q2. What law did the FTC allege the respondents violated?
Expected: Section 5(a) of the Federal Trade Commission Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45, through unfair and/or deceptive acts or practices.

Q3. When did Marriott acquire Starwood, and for how much?
Expected: Marriott announced the acquisition on or about November 16, 2015, and it closed on or about September 23, 2016, for $12.2 billion.

Q4. How many separate breaches does the complaint describe, and over what period?
Expected: Three separate breaches, between 2014 and 2020 (the First and Second Breaches in Starwood's network, and the Third Breach in Marriott's own network).

Q5. When did the First Breach begin, and how many consumers' payment card data was involved?
Expected: It began in June 2014 and continued for 14 months, involving payment card information for over 40,000 consumers.

Q6. How long did the Second Breach go undetected, and when was it detected?
Expected: The intruders were in the network for about four years (from on or about July 28, 2014); Marriott did not detect the Second Breach until September 7, 2018.

Q7. How many consumer records were compromised in the Second Breach, and how many unencrypted passport numbers?
Expected: 339 million consumer records globally, including more than 5.25 million unencrypted passport numbers.

Q8. List the security failures the FTC enumerated in Paragraph 27.
Expected: (a) appropriate password controls; (b) timely patching of outdated software; (c) adequate network monitoring and logging; (d) appropriate access controls; (e) appropriate firewall controls; (f) appropriate network segmentation; and (g) adequate multifactor authentication.

Q9. What did the Starwood and Marriott privacy policies represent about security?
Expected: Starwood's policy stated it safeguarded information using appropriate administrative, procedural, and technical safeguards including password controls, firewalls, and up to 256-bit encryption; Marriott's policy stated it sought to use reasonable organizational, technical, and administrative measures to protect personal data.

Q10. What were the two counts alleged?
Expected: Count I – Deceptive Security Statements (representing appropriate safeguards they did not use); Count II – Unfair Information Security Practices (failure to employ reasonable security causing or likely to cause substantial injury).

Q11. After the acquisition, who was responsible for the information security practices of both companies?
Expected: Marriott, which took control of Starwood's computer network and was responsible for establishing, reviewing, and implementing the information security practices for both itself and Starwood.

Q12. What happened in the Third Breach and whose network was involved?
Expected: Malicious actors compromised the credentials of employees at a Marriott-franchised property to access Marriott's own network; they accessed more than 5.2 million guest records (including 1.8 million U.S. consumer records), mainly to find loyalty accounts with redeemable points.

Q13. Why does the complaint say the harms were not reasonably avoidable by consumers?
Expected: Because consumers had no way to know about Respondents' information security failures described in Paragraph 27.

Q14. Who signed the complaint, and were any commissioners recused?
Expected: April J. Tabor, Secretary, by the Commission; Commissioners Holyoak and Ferguson were recused.

Q15. What did the forensic examiner find contributed to the First Breach?
Expected: Among other things, inadequate firewalls and network segmentation, inadequate access controls, the use of outdated and unsupported software, and the lack of multifactor authentication.
