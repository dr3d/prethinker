# FTC Complaint — In the Matter of Marriott International, Inc. and Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide, LLC (Docket No. C-4807)

> US Government work (Federal Trade Commission), public record. FTC Matter/File No. 192 3022; Docket No. C-4807; complaint issued December 20, 2024. Body text verbatim from the complaint; bracketed headings added as citation coordinates (the complaint's own section headings are retained); paragraph numbers preserved.

[CAPTION]
United States of America before the Federal Trade Commission. Commissioners: Lina M. Khan, Chair; Rebecca Kelly Slaughter; Alvaro M. Bedoya; Melissa Holyoak; Andrew Ferguson. In the Matter of Marriott International, Inc., a corporation, and Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide, LLC, a limited liability company. Docket No. C-4807. Complaint.

The Federal Trade Commission ("Commission"), having reason to believe that Marriott International, Inc., and Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide, LLC (collectively, "Respondents"), have violated the Federal Trade Commission Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45, and it appearing that this proceeding is in the public interest, alleges:

[PARTIES]
1. Respondent Marriott International, Inc. ("Marriott") is a Delaware corporation with its principal office at 7750 Wisconsin Ave., Bethesda, Maryland 20814.
2. Respondent Starwood Hotels & Resorts Worldwide, LLC ("Starwood") is a Maryland limited liability company with the same principal office. Starwood is a wholly-owned subsidiary of Marriott.
3. On or about September 23, 2016, acting alone or in concert with Starwood, Marriott formulated, directed, controlled, had the authority to control, or participated in the acts and practices set forth in this Complaint.
4. The acts or practices of Respondents have been in or affecting commerce and constitute unfair and/or deceptive acts or practices in violation of Section 5(a) of the FTC Act.

[RELEVANT BUSINESS PRACTICES]
5. Marriott is a multinational hospitality company that manages and franchises hotels, including 30 brands and more than 7,000 properties across the United States and 131 countries and territories.
6. On or about November 16, 2015, Marriott announced it would acquire Starwood for $12.2 billion; the acquisition closed on or about September 23, 2016, making Marriott the largest hotel chain in the world at that time, with over 1.1 million hotel rooms.
7. After the legal close of the acquisition, Marriott took control of Starwood's computer network and has been responsible for establishing, reviewing, and implementing the information security practices for both itself and Starwood. Marriott commenced a two-year integration of some Starwood systems and fully integrated them by December 2018.

[FIRST BREACH (STARWOOD)]
8. Approximately four days after Marriott's announcement of the acquisition, on or about November 20, 2015, Starwood notified consumers of a 14-month-long data breach involving payment card information for over 40,000 consumers (the "First Breach").
9. Beginning in June 2014 and continuing for 14 months, a malicious actor exploited security vulnerabilities to gain remote access to Starwood's network, compromised unprotected administrative accounts and credentials, and installed malware at more than one hundred Starwood properties. The forensic examination conducted by Starwood found that, among other things, inadequate firewalls and network segmentation, inadequate access controls, the use of outdated and unsupported software, and the lack of multifactor authentication contributed to the First Breach.
10. During the 10 months between the announcement of the acquisition and its closing, Marriott reviewed and evaluated Starwood's information security program, including the information security failures that led to the First Breach.

[SECOND BREACH (STARWOOD)]
11. Despite having responsibility for Starwood's information security practices and network following the acquisition, Marriott failed to identify an ongoing breach within the Starwood network (the "Second Breach"). Marriott did not detect the Second Breach until September 7, 2018, nearly two years after the close of the acquisition.
12. Forensic examiners determined that, on or about July 28, 2014, malicious actors compromised Starwood's external-facing webserver, installing malware that allowed the intruders to perform reconnaissance, harvest highly privileged administrative and user credentials, and move throughout Starwood's internal network for a four-year period, until Marriott's system detected an attempt to export consumer data on September 7, 2018.
13. Even after discovery, on September 10, 2018, the intruders exported additional guest information.
14. During this over-four-year period (July 2014 to September 2018), including the two years following Marriott's acquisition and integration, the intruders went undetected, installing key loggers, memory-scraping malware, and Remote Access Trojans in over 480 systems across 58 locations within the Starwood environment.
15. Following the Second Breach, Respondents' forensic examiner identified failures similar to those in the First Breach, including inadequate firewall controls, unencrypted payment card information stored outside of the secure cardholder data environment, lack of multifactor authentication, and inadequate monitoring and logging practices.
16. Due to the Second Breach, the personal information of 339 million consumer records globally was compromised, including more than 5.25 million unencrypted passport numbers, along with names, dates of birth, payment card numbers, contact details, loyalty numbers, and travel information.

[THIRD BREACH (MARRIOTT)]
17. The information security failures detailed in this Complaint are not limited to Starwood's networks, nor to the First and Second Breaches that began during Starwood's control of its information security program.
18. Marriott announced in March 2020 that malicious actors had compromised the credentials of employees at a Marriott-franchised property to gain access to Marriott's own network (the "Third Breach").
19. The intruders accessed and exported consumers' personal information without detection from September 2018 to December 2018, resumed in January 2020, and continued until discovered in February 2020.
20. The intruders accessed more than 5.2 million guest records, including 1.8 million records related to U.S. consumers, containing names, contact details, gender, partial dates of birth, Marriott loyalty account information, and hotel-stay preferences.
21. Marriott's internal investigation confirmed the malicious actors' main purpose was to identify loyalty accounts with sufficient points to be used or redeemed.

[DECEPTIVE SECURITY STATEMENTS]
22. Prior to its acquisition, Starwood controlled and operated www.starwood.com, where consumers could make reservations.
23. Following the acquisition, Marriott controlled and continued to operate the Starwood website until approximately May 2018, when it merged Starwood's website into the Marriott website.
24. At all relevant times, the privacy policy on the Starwood website stated that "All Starwood owned web sites and servers have security measures in place to help protect your personal data" and that "we safeguard your information using appropriate administrative, procedural and technical safeguards, including password controls, 'firewalls' and the use of up to 256-bit encryption."
25. Marriott also operates www.marriott.com, where consumers can make reservations for Marriott- and Starwood-branded hotels.
26. At all relevant times, the privacy policy on the Marriott website stated: "We seek to use reasonable organizational, technical and administrative measures to protect Personal Data."

[INFORMATION SECURITY PRACTICES]
27. Respondents failed to provide reasonable or appropriate security for the personal information that they collected and maintained about consumers. Among other things, Respondents:
a. Failed to implement appropriate password controls. As a result, employees often used default, blank, or weak passwords;
b. Failed to patch outdated software and systems in a timely manner, leaving Starwood's network susceptible to attacks; the forensic examiner for the First Breach noted unsupported operating systems for which patches were no longer available;
c. Failed to adequately monitor and log network environments, limiting the ability to detect malicious actors; this failure prevented Respondents from detecting intruders in their networks for several years during the Second Breach;
d. Failed to implement appropriate access controls; for example, the accounts of former employees were not terminated in a timely manner, and separate unique accounts for remote access were not created;
e. Failed to implement appropriate firewall controls, resulting in malicious actors making unauthorized connections from outside of Respondents' networks;
f. Failed to implement appropriate network segmentation, which allowed intruders to move easily between Starwood hotel property systems and Starwood's corporate network during multiple breaches; and
g. Failed to apply adequate multifactor authentication to protect sensitive information; for example, Starwood failed to comply with contractual obligations and internal policies requiring multifactor authentication for remote access to sensitive environments, including environments containing payment card data.

[CONSUMER INJURY]
28. As a direct result of the failures described in Paragraph 27, between 2014 and 2020 malicious actors gained unauthorized access to Respondents' networks in at least three separate breaches, exposing the personal information of millions of consumers. Such prolonged exposure has caused or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers.
29. For example, in the Third Breach, the theft of loyalty account numbers enabled malicious actors to fraudulently redeem loyalty points.
30. The exposure of more than 5.25 million unencrypted passport numbers in the Second Breach, combined with other personal information in the 339 million records, has caused or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers.
31. Consumers have suffered and will continue to suffer additional injuries, including wasted time and money to obtain identity theft protection, monitor accounts, replace passports, and replace compromised payment cards.
32. These harms were not reasonably avoidable by consumers, as consumers had no way to know about Respondents' information security failures described in Paragraph 27.

[VIOLATIONS — COUNT I: DECEPTIVE SECURITY STATEMENTS]
33. Through the means described in Paragraphs 24 and 26, Respondents have represented, expressly or by implication, that they used appropriate safeguards to protect consumers' personal information.
34. In truth and in fact, as described in Paragraph 27, Respondents did not use appropriate safeguards to protect consumers' personal information. Therefore, the representations set forth in Paragraphs 24 and 26 are false or misleading.

[VIOLATIONS — COUNT II: UNFAIR INFORMATION SECURITY PRACTICES]
35. As alleged in Paragraphs 27 to 32, Respondents' failure to employ reasonable security measures to protect consumers' personal information caused or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers that is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or competition and is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves. This practice was, and is, an unfair act or practice.

[VIOLATION OF SECTION 5]
36. The acts and practices of Respondents constitute unfair or deceptive acts or practices, in or affecting commerce, in violation of Section 5(a) of the Federal Trade Commission Act.

[SIGNATURE]
Therefore, the Federal Trade Commission this 20th day of December, 2024, has issued this complaint against Respondents. By the Commission, Commissioner Holyoak and Commissioner Ferguson recused. April J. Tabor, Secretary.
