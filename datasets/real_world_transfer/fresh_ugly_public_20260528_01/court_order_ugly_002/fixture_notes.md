# Fixture notes — court_order_ugly_002

## Why this document is messy
It is an appellate court order that, though short, packs the full apparatus of a federal opinion into a few pages: a caption with multiple party roles and counsel for each side, a three-judge panel issued per curiam, a procedural history that crosses three forums (Copyright Office -> Library of Congress -> MSPB -> Federal Circuit), an enumerated multi-prong standard of review quoted from a statute, a string of case citations with reporters and pin cites, two substantive footnotes, and a layered disposition (affirm; no authority to transfer; no costs; nonprecedential).

## What structures it pressures
- Party/role disambiguation: petitioner (pro se) vs. respondent (the MSPB itself, with two named counsel), plus the non-party Library of Congress and its Copyright Office.
- Identifier fidelity: the Federal Circuit appeal number (2025-1156) vs. the MSPB docket number (DC-3443-24-0906-I-1) vs. reporter citations.
- Date/sequence: a termination date, an initial-decision date, a separate "became final" date, and a decision date, spread across the caption and background.
- Citation strings: the standard-of-review statute, the jurisdictional statute, and four case citations with full reporters and pin cites, two of which appear only in footnotes.
- Source-state/limitation: the jurisdictional dismissal rationale, the no-authority-to-transfer point, the "no costs" line, and the nonprecedential designation.
- Cross-section reasoning: the holding rests on connecting the petitioner's argument (discussion) to precedent, and the procedural path spans caption, background, and conclusion.

## Known ambiguities in the source
- Two distinct dates attach to the Board's decision: the M.S.P.B. issuance date (October 4, 2024) and the date it "became final" (November 8, 2024); the opinion also says the Board acted "In October 2024."
- The case caption gives the petitioner's full name (Kevin Bailey Hornberger); the body uses "Mr. Hornberger."
- Footnote 2 discusses a case Hornberger cited (Strausbaugh) only to say it is not binding; it is not part of the holding, which can invite over-reading.
