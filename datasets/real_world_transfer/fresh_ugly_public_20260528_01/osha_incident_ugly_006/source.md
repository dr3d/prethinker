[DECISION AND ORDER]
During the early morning hours of November 12, 2022, a Precision Refractory Services, LLC, (PRS) employee was seriously injured when a scaffold component rolled off the upper level of the scaffold under which he had been working and struck his arm. The blow fractured his wrist. PRS reported the injury to the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) and the Mobile, Alabama, Area Office dispatched a Compliance Safety and Health Officer (CSHO) to the worksite to investigate the accident.
As a result of that investigation, the Secretary of Labor issued PRS a citation alleging two serious violations of regulations promulgated under § 5(a)(2) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970; 29 U.S.C. §§ 651-678 (2014) (the Act). The Secretary alleged PRS violated 29 C.F.R. §§ 1926.451(f)(7) (Item 1a) and 1926.451(h)(2)(i) (fn 1) (Item 1b), both of which address scaffold safety. The Secretary alleged PRS exposed its employee to a struck-by hazard by failing to ensure alterations to the scaffold were made by a competent person and by allowing employees to enter a hazardous area below the scaffold. The Secretary proposes a total penalty of $8702 for the violations. PRS timely contested the citation bringing this matter before the Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission pursuant to § 10(c) of the Act.
The undersigned held a hearing on January 6, 2025, in Mobile, Alabama. The parties filed post-hearing briefs on March 7, 2025 (fn 2).
For the reasons discussed below, Item 1a alleging a violation of § 1926.451(f)(7) is vacated, and Item 1b alleging a violation of § 1926.451(h)(2)(i) is affirmed with a penalty of $7000.

[JURISDICTION AND COVERAGE]
The parties stipulated jurisdiction over this action is conferred upon the Commission pursuant to § 10(c) of the Act. The parties also stipulated PRS was an employer engaged in a business affecting interstate commerce within the meaning of § 3(5) of the Act. The record establishes PRS is an employer covered under the Act and the Commission has jurisdiction over this proceeding.

[FINDINGS OF FACT]
PRS performs refractory and fireproofing work. Refractory work consists of laying fireproof or insulated brick inside heaters, boilers, furnaces, and other similar structures. PRS has been in business since 2021 and employs approximately 100 individuals.
In 2022, PRS had a contract to perform refractory work inside a furnace on the property of Ascend Performance Materials (Ascend) in Cantonment, Florida. The general contractor for the construction work being performed on the Ascend property was Koch (fn 4). Koch contracted with Coastal Industrial Services, Inc. (Coastal) to erect, maintain, and dismantle the scaffold required to perform the refractory work inside the furnace. Koch contracted with PRS to lay the fireproof brick inside the furnace. Since November 4, 2022, PRS had worked two 12-hour shifts or 24 hours each day on this contract.
The scaffolds were eight decks high with six feet between each deck. Coastal used a colored tag system to indicate scaffolds had been inspected and were safe to use. The accident occurred less than two weeks into the project. On the day of the accident, Domingo Rocha, a PRS foreman, and Jones Smith, a PRS laborer, were stacking brick on the scaffold at a height of approximately 12 to 14 feet above ground level. Juan Gallegos and another, unidentified, PRS employee were on the opposite side of the same scaffold doing similar work.
Jose Bustos, an onsite supervisor for PRS, had assigned the injured employee to work on the ground level below the scaffold cleaning up the falling debris while the other employees worked above. As the men worked, a scaffold component, described as a three-inch diameter pipe, fell off the scaffold to the ground below, striking the injured employee. Foreman Rocha descended the scaffold and contacted Supervisor Bustos, who took the injured employee to the onsite medical clinic. Andrew Castillo, the job superintendent for PRS, later transported the injured employee to a hospital where he was treated for an open fracture and laceration to his hand.
PRS reported the injury to the Mobile Area OSHA office. The Area Office assigned CSHO William Kitchen to perform an inspection of the worksite and investigation of the accident. CSHO Kitchen held an opening conference with representatives from Ascend, Koch, and PRS, took photographs from outside the furnace, obtained the names of employees present, and returned two days later to interview Foreman Rocha, Supervisor Bustos, and the unidentified employee. As a result of his investigation, CSHO Kitchen recommended the Secretary issue a citation alleging two serious violations of the scaffold standard.

[DISCUSSION — STANDARD]
To prove a violation of an OSHA standard, the Secretary must show by a preponderance of the evidence that (1) the cited standard applies; (2) the employer failed to comply with the terms of the cited standard; (3) employees had access to the violative condition; and (4) the cited employer either knew or could have known with the exercise of reasonable diligence of the violative condition. JPC Grp., Inc., No. 05-1907, 2009 WL 2567337, at *2 (OSHRC Aug. 11, 2009).

[DISCUSSION — ITEM 1A]
In Item 1a, the Secretary alleges a violation of § 1926.451(f)(7), which requires scaffolds be erected, moved, dismantled, or altered only under the supervision and direction of a competent person, and that such activities be performed only by experienced and trained employees selected by the competent person. PRS concedes it did not have a competent person on site to oversee construction or alteration of the scaffolds. The determinative issue is whether the Secretary established PRS employees altered the scaffolds. To prove noncompliance, the Secretary relied on statements CSHO Kitchen obtained from employees of Koch and Coastal; the court found these inadmissible hearsay and excluded them, rejecting the Secretary's argument that they fell within the Rule 807 "residual" exception because the statements lacked sufficient guarantees of trustworthiness and the Secretary failed to establish the declarants' unavailability. See Rivers v. United States, 777 F.3d 1306, 1312 (11th Cir. 2015). The remaining admitted evidence — CSHO Kitchen's testimony about an accusation by a Koch employee, and confusing testimony by Supervisor Bustos — was not probative of noncompliance, and the Secretary's circumstantial inference was matched by an equally reasonable alternative. Having failed to present persuasive, admissible evidence, the Secretary did not meet her burden to establish noncompliance. Item 1a, Citation 1, is vacated.

[DISCUSSION — ITEM 1B]
In Item 1b, the Secretary alleges PRS violated § 1926.451(h)(2)(i). Section 1926.451(h)(2) requires employee protection where there is a danger of tools, materials, or equipment falling from a scaffold and striking employees below, and sets forth several alternative means of compliance (barricading the area below, toeboards, panels/screening, guardrail systems, or canopies/debris nets/catch platforms). The conditions existed at the worksite, and the accident provides proof of actual exposure. PRS does not dispute it did not barricade the area below the scaffold and allowed employees to enter the area. PRS argued that because a toeboard would have prevented the accident, and because PRS could not install toeboards, it could not be in violation; the court rejected this reading, holding the standard's use of "or" establishes the enumerated methods as alternative means of compliance, and that an exposing employer has a statutory duty to protect its own employees even where it did not create or control the hazard. PRS's two supervisory employees (Supervisor Bustos and Foreman Rocha) knew an employee was working under the scaffold and were aware of the potential for objects to fall — Bustos had identified a falling-object hazard in his November 11th and 12th JSAs and knew the scaffolds lacked toeboards — and their knowledge is imputed to PRS. The Secretary established all elements; Item 1b, Citation 1, is affirmed.

[CHARACTERIZATION]
The Secretary alleges the violation was serious. A violation is serious when "there is a substantial probability that death or serious physical harm could result" from the hazardous condition. 29 U.S.C. § 666(k). The wrist fracture sustained by the injured employee evinces the serious nature of the hazard of objects falling 14 feet from the scaffold onto employees working below. The violation is serious.

[PENALTY]
The Commission must give due consideration to the gravity of the violation and to the size, history, and good faith of the employer under § 17(j) of the Act. The Secretary grouped the two alleged violations for penalty purposes and proposed a penalty of $8706. Because the court vacates Item 1a, the court will assess a gravity-based penalty for the single violation alleged in Item 1b. The gravity of the violation is high: although only one employee was exposed, he was exposed his entire shift; the likelihood that an object would fall was significant given multiple employees working above; and the injured employee sustained a fracture, a serious injury. The record reflects PRS is a small employer with no significant history of violations and was cooperative in the inspection, but contains no documentary evidence of training or safety and health programs to establish significant good-faith efforts. The court finds a penalty of $7000, approximately half the maximum penalty for a serious violation (fn 11), appropriate.

[ORDER]
Based upon the foregoing decision, it is ORDERED that: Item 1a, Citation 1, is vacated; Item 1b, Citation 1, is affirmed as a serious violation with a penalty of $7000 assessed. SO ORDERED.
