# fixture_notes — de_regulator_001

## Real features that make this document hard

- **German section-sign in legal citations.** „§ 53 Abs. 5 GWB" uses the section sign § (U+00A7) followed by a non-breaking space and the abbreviation „Abs." (Absatz, paragraph). Documents stripped of non-ASCII characters lose the § entirely; documents tokenized without the NBSP get „§53" or „§ 53Abs.5".
- **Brand-name with terminal exclamation as identity.** „FRITZ!" is the manufacturer's brand and the exclamation mark is part of the name, not punctuation. Removing terminal punctuation strips the brand identity to „FRITZ" — a different lexical object.
- **German date format DD.MM.YYYY.** „10.06.2024" and „02.07.2024" — the period separator is identical to a sentence-ending period and the day-month order is opposite of the US convention. „06.10.2024" would be October 6 not June 10; a US-trained parser silently inverts.
- **German compound nouns.** „Preisbeobachtungssoftware", „Elektronikfachhändler", „Bußgeldbescheid", „Hinweisgebersystem", „Verfahrensbeendigung". Each is a single lexical unit but pattern-matchers may split on internal morphemes („Preis-Beobachtung-s-Software", etc.).
- **Eszett and umlauts.** „Bußgeld" carries ß (U+00DF), and „Händler" / „bebußt" / „Düsseldorf" carry umlauts. ASCII-folding turns ß into „ss" (giving „Bussgeld") and strips umlauts (giving „Handler") — both are wrong as identifiers and break exact-match lookup.
- **„Mitte 2019" as a date approximation.** Not an exact date but a real document-level expression meaning roughly mid-year 2019. Systems that demand ISO-style dates either fabricate „2019-06" or reject the field.
- **Aktenzeichen format B10-21/23.** Bundeskartellamt case numbers use Decision-Division-Number/Year, with a single slash and a hyphen. The pattern „B10-21/23" looks like an arithmetic expression to a careless tokenizer.
- **Quotation marks: German-style „ … ".** The press release uses the low-9 opening (U+201E) and high-6 closing (U+201C) quotes for emphasized terms („FRITZ!", „Settlement"). ASCII normalizers replace both with the same ASCII straight quote, losing the directional pairing.
- **Defined-term mix of German and English.** „Settlement" appears as an English loanword in quotation marks inside a German sentence — a real BKartA practice. The press release does not translate „Bonus-Programm" / „Bußgeldbescheid" / „rechtskräftig" into English at any point; bilingual outputs require manual mapping.
- **Quoted statements from named officials.** The Mundt quote inside the press release is delimited by „ … ", carries a colon-introduction, and is in the first-person present subjunctive — a typical BKartA framing. The quote can be misattributed if the speaker tag is on a separate line.
- **Two-week appeal window implicit, not stated.** The press release relies on the reader knowing the Einspruchsfrist gegen Bußgeldbescheide is two weeks (§ 67 OWiG) — the period is invoked through the consequence („rechtskräftig") without naming the underlying provision.
- **Six retailers anonymized.** The six Elektronikfachhändler are not named — but each received its own Bußgeldbescheid. The aggregate „rund 16 Mio. EUR" includes both AVM's and the retailers' fines without separating them. A reader asking for AVM's individual fine cannot answer it from this document alone.
- **Geographic seat „Berlin" without further address.** AVM is identified by company name + city only — no street address, no court of registration, no HRB number. Identity disambiguation requires external lookup if multiple AVMs exist.
