# Fixture Notes — ntsb_marine_ugly_001

## Why this document was chosen

NTSB Marine Investigation Report MIR-25-21 is a completed investigation with a published probable cause — an important contrast to the still-open aviation fixture in this batch. It has dense weather sequencing (NWS watch → forecast → Special Marine Warning → updated warning → ground/buoy reports), a multi-actor event chain (Trinity Tugs ↔ Manson Construction Co. ↔ McDonough Marine Service ↔ U.S. Coast Guard sectors/stations/air station), and many overlapping numeric quantities (vessel dimensions, wind in mph vs. knots, distances in nautical miles, salvage and inspection dates spanning April through July 2024).

## Messy features

- **Time conventions**: the report explicitly states (footnote 1) "all times are central daylight time, and all miles are nautical miles unless otherwise noted." This means correct extraction requires reading the footnote and applying it to bare numerals throughout the document.
- **Two wind expressions**: the captain's estimate is stated as "85–100 mph (about 74–87 knots)" inline; the casualty summary gives the snapshot "winds southeast 23 kts, gusts 48 kts." The summary's wind direction (southeast) contradicts the body of the report, which describes the wind shifting to come "out of the north" — because the casualty summary captures the conditions in the area from a single observation, while the body describes the storm dynamics. A naive averaging or single-fact-per-question approach will miss this nuance.
- **Vessel had a prior name**: built 1997 as "Trent Joseph" for Coastal Towing LLC; renamed Baylor J. Tregre in 2023 by Trinity Tugs LLC. Questions about "who built" / "who owned" / "current operator" map differently.
- **Tow-line length contradiction across two passages**: the event sequence says "the main tow wire (towline), which was about 800 feet long"; the background paragraph says "1,800 feet of 1.25-inch tow wire" on the drum. Both are correct (800 ft was paid out; the drum had capacity for 1,800 ft). A question about "the towline length" must disambiguate.
- **Multiple Coast Guard entities** with different jurisdictions: Sector Houston-Galveston (received VHF distress call), District 8 New Orleans (received EPIRB), Station Freeport (launched response boat), Air Station Houston (launched MH-65 helicopter), and MSU Texas City (post-investigation cooperation). Different entities, different events, different times — easy to confuse.
- **Weather forecast vs. weather report ambiguity**: section 1.3.3.1 covers reported weather (after the fact, from KGLS/GNJT2/KGVW); section 1.3.3.2 covers forecasts issued before the casualty (NWS watch 1257, Coastal Waters Forecast 1409, Special Marine Warning 1501, update 1544). Each event has its own time. A question asking "when was the watch issued" must resolve to 1257, not 1501.
- **Counted entities**: 4 crewmembers, 5 main-deck exterior doors, 1 liferaft, 1 EPIRB, 2 deckhands, 2 diesel engines, 2 propellers. A counting question on "how many" requires picking the right noun.
- **Probable cause statement is causal but not a single fact**: it attributes the capsizing to "the mate's inability to maneuver the tow into the wind due to the overwhelming towline force generated by the towed barge during the sudden onset of severe weather, resulting in unrecoverable heeling." A logic extractor must preserve all three clauses (proximate-mechanical, intermediate-environmental, and outcome) without dropping one.
- **Counterfactual claim**: the Analysis says "even if the Baylor J. Tregre crew had received the Special Marine Warnings, they would not have been able to avoid the storm." This is a hypothetical the NTSB makes explicit; a question that asks whether earlier warning would have prevented the casualty must reflect the NTSB's stated counterfactual.
- **Multiple month timeline**: agreement May 11 → departure May 11 → southwesterly course change May 13 ~0630 → wind shift ~1647 → distress call 1657 → last AIS 1712 → EPIRB alert 1717 → response boat recovery 1808 → assist tug 2130 → marine warranty survey May 14 → tow wire cut May 15 → salvage May 18–June 15 → vessel raised to Galveston June 12 → NTSB exam July 9–10. Threading the timeline correctly is the central test.

## Prethinker design pressure

1. **Time-of-day arithmetic with footnote-resolved units**: the report establishes "all times CDT" once and assumes it throughout. Elapsed-time questions (e.g., from distress call to recovery) require operating on times in a consistent zone.
2. **Anaphora and role coreference**: the report repeatedly switches subjects between "the mate", "the captain", "deckhand 1", "deckhand 2". Reconstructing who did what requires resolving the watch schedule (captain + deckhand 1 vs. mate + deckhand 2 worked opposite watches).
3. **Source-record vs. analysis discrimination**: a value can appear both in the Casualty Summary (a one-row source record) and in the Analysis (with elaboration). The decimal lat/long format is only in the summary; the narrative uses miles-from-Galveston references.
4. **Counterfactual handling**: "even if X, Y would still have happened" is a different logical form than "X caused Y." A clean extractor cannot collapse them.
5. **Date entity disambiguation**: April 15 vs. April 22 (drydock vs. annual inspection vs. suitability survey, the latter two on the same date), with the suitability survey commissioned by a third party (the platform receiving company), not Trinity Tugs.
6. **Possessive-without-context references** in user follow-ups would map naturally to the vessel — "the crew", "the captain", "the doghouse" — all require resolving to the same Baylor J. Tregre case.

## Source caveats

- The report is the final NTSB product (probable cause adopted by the Board); not subject to further substantive revision.
- The terms "Gulf of America" and "Gulf of Mexico" reference the same body of water; the document uses "Gulf of America" throughout consistent with 2025 federal usage. Oracle answers preserve the document's wording.
- "Mph" vs. "knots" conversions in the report are author-supplied; the report sometimes presents one and sometimes both. The oracle uses the same unit the document uses for each instance.
