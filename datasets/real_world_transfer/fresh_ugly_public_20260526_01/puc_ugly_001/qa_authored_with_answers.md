**4. What is the statutory deadline within which the Commission must reach a final decision on a completed rate case petition?**

**Reference answer.**
10 months from the filing of the completed petition or application. The deadline is set by MCL 460.6a(5), originally enacted via Public Act 341 of 2016. The order quotes the statutory provision: 'if the commission fails to reach a final decision with respect to a completed petition or application to increase or decrease utility rates within the 10-month period following the filing of the completed petition or application, the petition or application is considered approved.' The deadline 'can be extended, but only at the request of the filing utility.' Prior to Act 341, the timeline was 12 months; Act 341 reduced it to 10 months.

**22. What happens if the Commission fails to reach a final decision on a completed rate case petition within the statutory 10-month window?**

**Reference answer.**
The petition or application is 'considered approved' — i.e., a default-approval consequence per MCL 460.6a(5). Quoted verbatim from the order: 'if the commission fails to reach a final decision with respect to a completed petition or application to increase or decrease utility rates within the 10-month period following the filing of the completed petition or application, the petition or application is considered approved.' This is subject to 'limited exceptions' (not enumerated in the order). The timeline can be extended only at the filing utility's request — the Commission cannot unilaterally extend. The Commission notes this as one of the aspects of the rate case process not within its control.

**24. The order cites both Public Act 231 of 2023 and Public Act 341 of 2016 as relevant statutory frameworks. What is the relationship between the two acts, and how does each appear in the order's regulatory analysis?**

**Reference answer.**
Public Act 231 of 2023 (Act 231) is the mandate — its § 6aa(3) directed the Commission to open this investigation. Act 231 became effective February 13, 2024 (signed November 28, 2023). The Commission's May 23, 2024 opening of Case U-21637 is the response to that mandate (with the 'June 1, 2024' deadline in the statute). Public Act 341 of 2016 (Act 341) is the underlying problem framing — its § 6a(5) reduced the rate case decision window from 12 months to 10 months. The 8-year span of operating under Act 341's compressed timeline is what created the case-record-expansion pressure that motivates this investigation (per the Commission's third body paragraph). So: Act 341 (2016) → 10-month constraint → 8 years of case expansion → Act 231 (2023) → mandated investigation → present order. Both statutes amend or operate under MCL 460.6a (codified). Act 341 changed § 6a(5); Act 231 added § 6aa(3). The two acts work in tandem.

**Source coordinates.** Opening paragraphs of the body; throughout discussion

**Pressure tags.** two_acts_one_codified_chapter, mandate_vs_underlying_constraint, eight_year_intervening_period, section_amendment_vs_section_addition

**25. The Commission "agreed with Ann Arbor, DTE, the Attorney General, and the Staff" on the Demand Response process question, while declining to follow Consumers' proposal. Identify the specific staff proposal that was adopted, and explain why Consumers' alternative was not selected.**

**Reference answer.**
The Staff offered TWO alternatives. The Commission adopted the SECOND staff proposal: 'DR could be moved entirely into the rate case... each utility would need to file a final DR reconciliation wherein a final balance would be determined and recovered in a subsequent rate case. This would allow the reconciliation process to end and all DR costs to be included in subsequent rate cases.' The Commission's reasoning: (a) rate cases allow for holistic analysis, (b) DR is more akin to generation investments and should be part of holistic review (with capacity amounts determined in IRPs), (c) authorization of DR spending is already considered in rate cases for all applicable utilities. Consumers' proposal — 'one combined annual DR plan and reconciliation case' with surcharge collection similar to Energy Waste Reduction (EWR) — was rejected because (per the Attorney General's argument that the Commission agreed with): 'Consumers' proposal would continue the current bifurcation of proceedings and the unnecessary use of additional resources.' The Commission also disagreed with MEGA's flexibility proposal but noted MEGA proposed no alternative. Operative Provision A implements the chosen path: post-three-part-process reconciliation, DR cost requests flow into general rate cases.

**Source coordinates.** Demand Response Process discussion; operative Provision A

**Pressure tags.** adoption_of_second_of_two_staff_alternatives, rejection_of_consumers_combined_proposal, holistic_review_rationale, ag_argument_against_bifurcation
