**15. List the four examples (by section, paragraph, or named subsection) where the letter says the firm's response was "inadequate" — and for each, give one stated reason why.**

1. CGMP Violation #1 (microbiological contamination): the risk assessment did not extend to all drug products where (b)(4) treatment was attempted; did not address bacterial-species identification or evidence that yeast/mold plate growth was solely bacterial.
2. CGMP Violation #2 (process validation): the firm did not evaluate the implications of changes made to product formulations.
3. CGMP Violation #3 (investigations / *Complaint Investigations*): the firm committed to revising complaint and investigation procedures but did not provide the revised procedures; did not perform a retrospective review of invalidated OOS results or address improperly invalidated results for products still on the market.
4. CGMP Violation #4 (stability): the response did not include a comprehensive review of the stability program, did not provide the revised stability protocol, and did not describe a retrospective review of batches that lack adequate stability data.

## Obligations / violations / consequences / corrective actions

**21. List the potential consequences identified in the "Conclusion" section if the firm fails to promptly and adequately address the violations.**

From the Conclusion paragraphs, the explicitly named possible consequences are:

- Regulatory or legal action without further notice, including seizure and injunction.
- Other Federal agencies may be prevented from awarding contracts to the firm.
- FDA may withhold issuance of Export Certificates.
- FDA may withhold approval of new applications or supplements listing the firm as a drug manufacturer.
- FDA may re-inspect to verify completion of corrective actions.

**22. What corrective and preventive action (CAPA) did the firm propose in response to the microbiological contamination under CGMP Violation #1, and why does FDA describe it as inadequate?**

The firm's CAPA included revising the drug product formulation to increase (b)(4) content and committing to no longer allow (b)(4) treatment to rework batches after failing microbiological results, and to place a reworked batch on real-time stability; the firm also submitted a risk assessment concluding no health risk for two failing lots. FDA found this inadequate because: (a) the risk assessment did not extend to all drug products where (b)(4) treatment was attempted; (b) the firm did not address product quality and patient safety risks for lots on which (b)(4) treatment was used or product released after failing micro results; and (c) the firm did not address whether additional bacterial species were present or provide evidence that growth on total yeast/mold plates was solely bacterial. FDA also notes that (b)(4) "are not a substitute for a comprehensive approach to preventing objectionable microorganisms."

**23. What labeling claim is given for Hydro-Q Gel (hydroquinone 4%) in the "Unapproved New Drug Violations" section?**

"Indicated for the gradual bleaching of hyperpigmented skin conditions such as chloasma, melasma, freckles, senile lentigines and areas of melanin hyperpigmentation."

## Cross-section

**24. Under CGMP Violation #1, the letter notes a voluntary recall. What date was the recall issued, what product was recalled, and which lots were affected?**

The letter states only: "We note that on (b)(4), you issued a voluntary recall of (b)(4), lots (b)(4), due to microbiological contamination." The recall date, the product name, and the affected lot identifiers are all redacted as (b)(4) — they cannot be answered from the public letter alone. The only fact recoverable is the *cause* of the recall: microbiological contamination.

**25. The letter is signed by two FDA officials. Based on the offices they each direct, which signatory is responsible for the unapproved-new-drugs portion of the letter and which is responsible for the CGMP-violations portion?**

The letter has two signature blocks at the close:

- Francis Godwin, Director, Office of Manufacturing Quality (within Office of Compliance, CDER) — corresponds to the CGMP-violations portion (Violations #1–#4 under 21 CFR Part 211).
- Tina Smith, Captain, U.S. Public Health Service, Director, Office of Unapproved Drugs & Labeling Compliance (within Office of Compliance, CDER) — corresponds to the "Unapproved New Drug Violations" section (sections 301(d) and 505(a) of the FD&C Act).

The mapping is inferred from the office names: "Manufacturing Quality" → CGMP, "Unapproved Drugs & Labeling Compliance" → unapproved-new-drug labeling claims.
