**9. The letter cites 21 U.S.C. § 321(rr). What FD&C Act section does that correspond to, and what definition does it provide?**

It corresponds to section 201(rr) of the FD&C Act. It defines "tobacco product" to include products that are made or derived from tobacco or contain nicotine from any source and are intended for human consumption. As amended on March 15, 2022, the definition expressly covers products "containing nicotine from any source."

## Dates / deadlines / duration / sequence

**16. What is the date the letter is dated, and what is the "Content current as of" date posted on the FDA web page?**

Letter date: June 6, 2025. "Content current as of": 06/17/2025 (June 17, 2025).

**17. How many calendar days elapsed between the letter's signature date and the "Content current as of" date?**

11 calendar days (June 6, 2025 → June 17, 2025).

**18. By when must the firm submit its written response, and from what trigger date is that period measured?**

Within 15 working days from the date of receipt of the letter (not from the date of the letter itself).

**19. What date does the letter give as the cutoff for a product to be considered a "new tobacco product"?**

February 15, 2007. Any tobacco product not commercially marketed in the U.S. as of that date — or modified after that date — is a "new tobacco product" under section 910(a) of the FD&C Act.

**20. According to the letter, on what date was federal legislation signed that expanded FDA jurisdiction to products containing nicotine from any source?**

March 15, 2022. The Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2022 (Public Law 117-103, Division P, Title I, Subtitle B).

## Obligations / violations / consequences / corrective actions

**21. List every possible enforcement action mentioned in the letter that FDA may take if violations are not adequately addressed.**

- Civil money penalties
- Seizure
- Injunction
- Detention or refusal of admission of imported tobacco products

The letter explicitly notes that the Warning Letter itself does **not** constitute "written notice" for purposes of section 303(f)(9)(B)(i)(II) of the FD&C Act, which is a qualification on the civil-money-penalty pathway.

**22. The letter states that these ENDS products are "adulterated" and "misbranded." Under which sub-sections of the FD&C Act are each of these determinations made?**

- Adulterated under section 902(6)(A) of the FD&C Act (21 U.S.C. § 387b(6)(A)) — because the products lack a marketing authorization order under section 910(c)(1)(A)(i).
- Misbranded under section 903(a)(6) of the FD&C Act (21 U.S.C. § 387c(a)(6)) — because a section 905(j) notice was not provided.

**23. What two pieces of information about the firm's response must be provided beyond stating actions taken (i.e., what other content does the letter require the response to include)?**

The response must include (i) the dates on which the firm discontinued the violative sale and/or distribution of the listed tobacco products, and (ii) the firm's plan for maintaining compliance with the FD&C Act going forward.

It also notes that if the firm believes the products are not in violation, it should include its reasoning and any supporting information for FDA's consideration.

## Cross-section

**24. The recipient header lists "vaping.com" as the recipient, but the cc list reveals additional related entities. What is the legal business entity associated with vaping.com per the cc list, and what other "d/b/a" name does that entity also operate under?**

The legal entity is SV3, LLC. Per the cc list:
- SV3, LLC d/b/a Vaping.com — at 4908 E McDowell Road, Ste 103
- SV3, LLC d/b/a Smoking Vapor — at 2303 N 44th St, Ste 11

So SV3, LLC operates under two relevant d/b/a names: "Vaping.com" and "Smoking Vapor." It also appears under its bare name (SV3, LLC) at additional addresses. The web hosting (GoDaddy.com) and storefront platform (Shopify, Inc.) are cc'd as separate non-affiliated entities at abuse@ addresses, indicating the platforms are being notified but are not themselves the subject of the violations.

**25. The letter cites both section 902(6)(A) for adulteration and section 903(a)(6) for misbranding. Looking across the letter, which procedural failure (under section 905(j)) is the underlying cause cited for the misbranding determination?**

The misbranding finding is based on the firm's failure to provide a notice (or other information) under section 905(j) of the FD&C Act (21 U.S.C. § 387e(j)) — a substantial-equivalence report. Section 905(j) is also referenced earlier in the letter as one of the two procedural alternatives to a full premarket marketing authorization order under section 910(c)(1)(A)(i). The same statutory section thus appears in both the "what's required" framing (early in the letter) and the "what's missing" finding (in the adulteration/misbranding paragraph) — joining those two parts of the letter.
