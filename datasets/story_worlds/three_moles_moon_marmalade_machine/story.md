# The Three Moles and the Moon-Marmalade Machine

Once upon a time there were three Moles who lived together in a kettle-house of their own, under an elderberry bank beside a crooked canal. One was a Little Nib Mole, and one was a Middle Muddle Mole, and the other was a Great Burrow Mole.

They had each a cup for drinking root-tea; a little cup for the Little Nib Mole, a middle-sized cup for the Middle Muddle Mole, and a great cup for the Great Burrow Mole. And they had each a cap for wearing when the ceiling dripped; a little cap for the Little Nib Mole, a middle-sized cap for the Middle Muddle Mole, and a great cap for the Great Burrow Mole. And they had each a cart for carrying useful underground things; a little cart for the Little Nib Mole, a middle-sized cart for the Middle Muddle Mole, and a great cart for the Great Burrow Mole.

Now these Moles were careful Moles, though they were very near-sighted, as Moles often are, and they kept labels on nearly everything except themselves. One morning they made moon-marmalade for breakfast in their humming marmalade machine. They made it with crabapples, star-sugar, and seven little silver beetle-keys, because ordinary marmalade is good for toast, but moon-marmalade is good for gloomy Tuesdays.

When the marmalade was done, it glowed so brightly that the Great Burrow Mole said, “Dear tunnels! If we eat it now, it will shine straight through our whiskers.”

So they poured the moon-marmalade into three jars: a little jar for the Little Nib Mole, a middle-sized jar for the Middle Muddle Mole, and a great jar for the Great Burrow Mole. Then they went out to the foggy meadow to fetch three fog-leaves to dim the glow: a little fog-leaf, a middle-sized fog-leaf, and a great fog-leaf.

While they were gone, there came by a girl named Mina Moonbutton, who had been sent by her uncle to buy brown twine and vinegar. But Mina had been counting doorbells, chasing a backward frog, and listening to a fence that squeaked in rhymes, so she had not bought the brown twine, and she had almost forgotten the vinegar except for a vinegary feeling in her nose.

Presently she saw the kettle-house under the elderberry bank.

First she peered through the spoon-slot.

Then she put her ear to the drainpipe.

Then she knocked on the door with her left glove, so softly that even the glove was not sure it had knocked.

Nobody answered.

Now, if Mina Moonbutton had been a tidy-minded child, she would have waited outside and called, “Good morning, Moles, I am lost and probably sticky.” But Mina was not tidy-minded. She was a child who thought that “Do not touch” meant “Touch in an educational manner.” So she lifted the kettle-latch and went inside.

There on the table were the three jars of moon-marmalade.

First Mina opened the great jar of the Great Burrow Mole and tasted a spoonful. But that marmalade was too bright. It lit up both her ears from the inside, and she could see the bones of her hat.

“This marmalade is too shiny,” said Mina.

Next she opened the middle-sized jar of the Middle Muddle Mole and tasted a spoonful. But that marmalade was too dim. It tasted like bedtime, and for a moment Mina forgot whether she was Mina Moonbutton, Moon Mina Button, or Button Mina Moon.

“This marmalade is too sleepy,” said Mina.

Then she opened the little jar of the Little Nib Mole and tasted a spoonful. That marmalade was neither too shiny nor too sleepy, but glimmered in a friendly, lanterny way. Mina liked it so well that she ate the little jar all up, every glowing bit, including the seven little silver beetle-keys.

After that her hiccups began to hum.

“Hm!” hiccupped Mina.

“Hum!” hiccupped Mina.

“Hum-ding!” hiccupped Mina, which surprised her very much.

Then Mina saw the three caps hanging on three pegs.

First she put on the great cap of the Great Burrow Mole, but it spun around and around until the whole room became a pudding.

“This cap is too dizzy,” said Mina.

Then she put on the middle-sized cap of the Middle Muddle Mole, but it announced every thought in her head.

“Mina is thinking about touching another thing!” cried the cap.

“This cap is too tell-tale,” said Mina, and she took it off quickly.

Then she put on the little cap of the Little Nib Mole, and it was neither too dizzy nor too tell-tale, but just secret enough. It whispered, “The pantry is interesting,” which was true but not helpful.

So Mina followed the whispering cap into the pantry, where she backed into a shelf and knocked down a jar of pickled thunder. The thunder rolled about the floor in small blue lumps, grumbling.

“That shelf jumped,” said Mina, though it had done no such thing.

After that Mina thought she might like to ride in one of the carts.

First she climbed into the great cart of the Great Burrow Mole, but it rolled uphill backward and would not stop bowing to itself.

“This cart is too proud,” said Mina.

Next she climbed into the middle-sized cart of the Middle Muddle Mole, but it would not move until she signed a delivery receipt, a permission slip, and a statement explaining why she was in a kettle-house without any twine.

“This cart is too official,” said Mina.

Then she climbed into the little cart of the Little Nib Mole, and it was neither too proud nor too official, but just brisk enough. Mina took hold of the handles and pretended she was Postmistress of the Moon.

But the little cart was made for Moles carrying turnips through tunnels, not for girls delivering imaginary moon-mail in pantries. So it began to dig.

Down it went through the pantry floor, under the clock-cupboard, and straight into the box where the Moles kept their spare minutes. Out spilled all the minutes: breakfast minutes, broom minutes, apology minutes, and three very expensive Sunday minutes.

The little cart tipped over. Mina tumbled into the flour-bin, and the little cap slid over one eye. A blue lump of pickled thunder hopped into her pocket and grumbled there.

By this time the three Moles had gathered their fog-leaves and were coming home.

The Great Burrow Mole opened the door and squinted at the table.

“Somebody has opened my great jar!” said the Great Burrow Mole in his great burrowy voice.

“And somebody has opened my middle jar!” said the Middle Muddle Mole in her middle muddly voice.

“And somebody has eaten my little jar all up!” squeaked the Little Nib Mole in his little nibby voice.

Then they looked at their caps.

“Somebody has spun my cap!” said the Great Burrow Mole.

“And somebody has made mine gossip!” said the Middle Muddle Mole.

“And somebody has followed mine into trouble!” squeaked the Little Nib Mole.

Then they looked at their carts.

“Somebody has rolled in my cart!” said the Great Burrow Mole.

“And somebody has refused to fill out my cart’s forms!” said the Middle Muddle Mole.

“And somebody has dug a hole with my cart and spilled our minutes!” cried the Little Nib Mole.

At that moment Mina hiccupped from inside the flour-bin.

“Hum-ding!”

The Moles looked in and found her white with flour, bright about the ears, wearing the little cap sideways, with pickled thunder grumbling in her pocket, and her middle going hum, hum-ding, hum-ding-dong.

The Great Burrow Mole said, “That is not a sensible sound for a child.”

The Middle Muddle Mole said, “Nor properly scheduled.”

And the Little Nib Mole said, “She has swallowed my beetle-keys.”

Mina began to cry little floury tears.

“I am sorry,” she said. “I was sent for brown twine and vinegar, but I found a humming breakfast instead.”

“That,” said the Great Burrow Mole, “is a crooked road to vinegar.”

Then the Middle Muddle Mole gave Mina a napkin. The Little Nib Mole gave her a very small frown. And the Great Burrow Mole fetched the winding fork from the marmalade machine.

They stood Mina on the flour barrel and wound her gently with the winding fork until the seven silver beetle-keys hopped out of her mouth, one by one, each saying “ting!” The last one had a tooth mark, but it still worked.

After that Mina helped sweep the flour, gather the spilled minutes, mend the pantry floor, polish the caps, and put the pickled thunder back in its jar.

Then, because the Moles were careful but not cruel, they gave her ordinary toast with ordinary marmalade on it, which did not hum, shine, dig, announce, or require winding.

When Mina finally went home, she had brown twine, vinegar, a fog-leaf, and a promise in her pocket never again to enter a kettle-house without being asked.

And ever after, when Mina felt tempted to touch a thing in an educational manner, she heard a tiny sound in her memory:

“Hum-ding.”

Which meant:

“Ask first, or breakfast may answer.”
