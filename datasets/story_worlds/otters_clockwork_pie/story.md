## The Three Otters and the Clockwork Pie

Once upon a time there were three Otters, who lived together in a snug house of their own beneath the roots of a willow-tree, by the side of a bright running river. One of them was a Little Slip of an Otter, and one was a Middle-sized Otter, and the other was a Great Long Otter.

They had each a mug for their river-tea; a little mug for the Little Slip of an Otter, and a middle-sized mug for the Middle-sized Otter, and a great mug for the Great Long Otter. And they had each a pair of boots for walking upon muddy banks; little boots for the Little Slip of an Otter, middle-sized boots for the Middle-sized Otter, and great boots for the Great Long Otter. And they had each a boat for rowing in the rain-barrel; a little boat for the Little Slip of an Otter, a middle-sized boat for the Middle-sized Otter, and a great boat for the Great Long Otter.

Now these Otters were tidy Otters, as Otters go; which is to say that their spoons were always in the spoon-cup, though their tails were sometimes in the soup. One morning they baked a clockwork pie for breakfast, made of apples, moon-sugar, and six little brass wheels, for it was a special day, being the Little Slip of an Otter's half-birthday. But the pie came out of the oven ticking very fiercely, and the Great Long Otter said-

"Dear me! If we eat it now it will tickle our whiskers off."

So the three Otters put the clockwork pie upon the windowsill to cool, and went down to the river to gather three sprigs of mint; a little sprig for the Little Slip of an Otter, a middle-sized sprig for the Middle-sized Otter, and a great dripping sprig for the Great Long Otter.

While they were gone there came by a small person named Tilly Tumbletop, who had been sent by her aunt to fetch a spool of blue thread and a pennyworth of pepper. But Tilly had been poking sticks into puddles, and counting beetles, and following a cloud that looked like a goose; so she had quite forgotten the blue thread and had only a hazy notion of pepper.

Presently she saw the Otters' house under the willow-roots.

First she looked in at the round window.

Then she peeped through the chimney-hole.

Then she tapped the door with one finger, very softly, so that if anyone answered she could say she had knocked, and if no one answered she could say she had hardly knocked at all.

Nobody answered.

Now, if Tilly Tumbletop had been a properly polished child, she would have waited outside and said, "Good morning, Otters, I believe I am lost." But she was not polished; she was only rubbed about at the edges. So she lifted the latch and went in.

There upon the windowsill was the clockwork pie.

"Oh!" cried Tilly. "A pie that ticks! That must be a pie in a hurry."

She took the knife of the Great Long Otter and cut herself a great slice, but that slice went **tick-tock-tick-tock** so fast that it chased her fork all around the plate.

"This pie is too quick," said Tilly.

Then she took the knife of the Middle-sized Otter and cut herself a middle-sized slice, but that slice went **tock... tick... tock... tick...** so slowly that she fell asleep waiting for the raisins to arrive.

"This pie is too slow," said Tilly.

Then she took the little knife of the Little Slip of an Otter and cut herself a little slice, and that slice went **tick-tock, tick-tock** in such a merry, comfortable fashion that Tilly liked it very much indeed. So she ate it all up, crust, crumbs, wheels, and every bit.

After that she felt a curious buzzing in her stomach, for one should never eat the brass wheels out of another person's breakfast.

Then Tilly saw the three pairs of boots.

First she put on the great boots of the Great Long Otter, but they were so large that each boot walked in a different direction and took her legs with them.

"These boots are too wandering," said Tilly.

Next she put on the middle-sized boots of the Middle-sized Otter, but they were so sensible and stiff that they would not move at all unless she first stated her errand clearly, which she could not do.

"These boots are too particular," said Tilly.

Then she put on the little boots of the Little Slip of an Otter, and they were neither too wandering nor too particular, but just mischievous enough. They began to dance; and Tilly danced after them, round the table, over the mat, under the stool, and finally straight into the pantry, where she knocked down a jar of sugared minnows.

"That was not my fault," said Tilly, though it mostly was.

After dancing so much, she thought she would like a quiet row in one of the boats. So she climbed into the great boat of the Great Long Otter, which sat in the rain-barrel, but it rocked so grandly that it splashed water into both her ears.

"This boat is too billowy," said Tilly.

Then she climbed into the middle-sized boat of the Middle-sized Otter, but it had so many rules painted on the inside that she had to read them before rowing, and Rule Number Seven said: **No rowing until all previous rules have been obeyed.**

"This boat is too bossy," said Tilly.

Then she climbed into the little boat of the Little Slip of an Otter, and that boat was neither too billowy nor too bossy, but just right. So she took up the little oars and rowed round and round the rain-barrel, pretending she was queen of a lake.

But the little boat had not been made for queens. It had been made for Otters. And Tilly was heavier than an Otter, especially after eating a slice of clockwork pie. So the little boat sank lower and lower, until the rain-barrel gave a great **glug**, the plug popped out, and all the water rushed across the floor.

The little boat spun three times, turned over, and left Tilly sitting in a puddle with a sugared minnow stuck to her sleeve.

By this time the three Otters had gathered their mint and were coming home.

The Great Long Otter opened the door and sniffed.

"Somebody has been cutting my clockwork pie!" said the Great Long Otter, in his great long voice.

"And somebody has been cutting my clockwork pie!" said the Middle-sized Otter, in her middle-sized voice.

"And somebody has eaten my little slice all up!" squeaked the Little Slip of an Otter, in his little slip of a voice.

Then the Great Long Otter looked at his boots.

"Somebody has been walking in my boots!"

"And somebody has been arguing with my boots!" said the Middle-sized Otter.

"And somebody has danced holes in mine!" squeaked the Little Slip of an Otter.

Then they saw the rain-barrel.

"Somebody has been rowing in my boat!"

"And somebody has been ignoring the rules in my boat!"

"And somebody has sunk my boat all the way to the bottom!" cried the Little Slip of an Otter.

At that moment Tilly Tumbletop sneezed from inside the pantry.

The Otters looked in.

There she sat, dripping wet, with her hair full of crumbs, her pockets full of sugared minnows, and her stomach going **tick-tock, tick-tock, tick-tock**.

For a moment nobody spoke.

Then the Great Long Otter said, "That is not a healthy sound for a child."

The Middle-sized Otter said, "Nor a lawful one."

And the Little Slip of an Otter said, "She has got my half-birthday wheels inside her."

Tilly began to cry, not loudly, but in the leaky sort of way, like a teapot with feelings.

"I am sorry," she said. "I was sent for blue thread and pepper, and I found a ticking pie instead."

"Well," said the Great Long Otter, "that is how trouble often begins."

The Middle-sized Otter gave her a towel. The Little Slip of an Otter gave her a look, which was smaller than the towel but sharper.

Then they did a very clever thing. They stood Tilly upon a stool and wound her gently with the pie-key until the six brass wheels came clicking out of her mouth, one by one, quite clean and none the worse except for one tooth-mark.

After that, Tilly helped mop the floor. She helped mend the little boat. She helped polish the boots. And then, because the Otters were hospitable Otters, though a little dampened in their opinion of visitors, they gave her a fresh slice of ordinary pie, without any machinery in it.

When Tilly finally went home, she had the blue thread, the pepper, a sprig of mint, and a very firm promise never again to enter a willow-root house without being asked.

And ever after, when she felt tempted to meddle with things that were not hers, she heard a tiny sound inside her memory:

**Tick-tock. Tick-tock.**

Which meant:
"Mind your manners before the pie does it for you."
