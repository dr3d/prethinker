# The Kestrel Claim

## Source Document: Combined Record of Maritime Insurance Dispute, Survey Reports, Reinsurance Trace, Arbitration Proceedings, and Regulatory Correspondence

### SECTION A — Insurance and Reinsurance Structure

#### A.1 — The Vessel

The M/V Kestrel is a 2018-built bulk carrier, 82,000 DWT, Panama-flagged,
classed with Bureau Veritas (BV), managed by Meridian Ship Management Ltd
(Cyprus), and beneficially owned by Kestrel Maritime Holdings SA (Liberia).
The vessel's Classification Society survey record was current as of
September 15, 2025 (annual survey, no conditions of class outstanding).

#### A.2 — Hull and Machinery Policy

The Kestrel is insured under a Hull and Machinery (H&M) policy, Policy No.
HM-KES-2025-0417, written on the International Hull Clauses (IHC) 2003 form.
The policy period runs from January 1, 2025 to December 31, 2025. The agreed
insured value is USD 28,500,000.

The lead underwriter is Oceanic Syndicate 1847 at Lloyd's of London (45% line).
Following underwriters are Nordic Marine Insurance AS, Oslo (30% line) and
Harbour Mutual P&I, Piraeus (25% line).

The policy contains a Classification Clause requiring the vessel to maintain
class with an IACS member society throughout the policy period. Loss of class
or the imposition of a condition of class that is not rectified within the
period specified by the classification society suspends cover automatically
from the date of loss or imposition, not from the date the underwriters are
notified. Cover resumes only upon reinstatement of class in good standing and
written confirmation from the lead underwriter.

The policy also contains a Trading Warranties clause. The vessel is warranted
not to trade to or from any port subject to EU, US, or UN sanctions. Breach of
the trading warranty discharges the underwriters from liability from the date of
breach, unless the breach is remedied before a loss occurs and the assured can
show that the breach did not contribute to the loss (per Section 34 of the UK
Marine Insurance Act 1906, as modified by the Insurance Act 2015).

The deductible is USD 150,000 per occurrence for machinery damage and USD
250,000 per occurrence for grounding or stranding.

#### A.3 — Reinsurance Structure

Oceanic Syndicate 1847 reinsures its 45% line as follows:

- First layer: 70% of Oceanic's net retained line (i.e., 70% of 45% = 31.5%
  of insured value) is ceded to Pinnacle Re, Bermuda, under Excess of Loss
  Treaty PRT-2025-112. Pinnacle Re's attachment point is USD 500,000 net to
  Oceanic, with a limit of USD 5,000,000 any one vessel.

- Second layer: above Pinnacle Re's USD 5,000,000 limit, excess is ceded to
  a panel of retrocessionaires led by Fortis Re, Zurich, under Retrocession
  Certificate FRC-2025-044. Fortis Re's attachment point is USD 5,000,000
  excess of USD 500,000 (i.e., USD 5,500,000 net to Oceanic), with a limit
  of USD 8,000,000.

Nordic Marine Insurance AS retains its 30% line net, with no facultative
reinsurance.

Harbour Mutual P&I's 25% line is backed by its own club pooling arrangement
and is not separately reinsured for H&M purposes, though Harbour Mutual has
an Overspill facility through the International Group of P&I Clubs for
liabilities exceeding USD 10,000,000 per vessel.

Pinnacle Re's treaty contains a 72-hour notice provision: the reassured
(Oceanic) must notify the reinsurer of any claim or circumstance likely to
give rise to a claim within 72 hours of the reassured becoming aware of it.
Late notification does not automatically void coverage but entitles the
reinsurer to reduce its liability by the amount of prejudice caused by the
late notice, which must be proved by the reinsurer.

#### A.4 — Protection and Indemnity (P&I) Coverage

Third-party liabilities (crew injury, pollution, cargo damage, wreck removal)
are covered separately by Harbour Mutual P&I acting in its P&I club capacity.
The P&I cover is not part of the Hull and Machinery policy and is subject to
the club rules, including a requirement that the member (Kestrel Maritime
Holdings) must notify the club of any incident likely to give rise to a P&I
claim within 14 days of the incident. The P&I club year runs from February 20
to February 20 (noon GMT), not calendar year.

Harbour Mutual thus sits on both sides of this loss: as an H&M following
underwriter (25% line on Policy HM-KES-2025-0417) and as the P&I insurer for
third-party liabilities. The two roles are governed by separate contracts with
separate terms, separate notification requirements, and separate deductibles.

### SECTION B — The Incident

**October 12, 2025, 03:17 UTC** — M/V Kestrel, laden with 74,000 MT of
Brazilian soybean meal, grounds on an uncharted submerged obstruction while
transiting the Bight of Benin, approximately 4.2 nautical miles southeast of
Lomé, Togo. The vessel is on a voyage from Santos, Brazil to Lagos, Nigeria.

**October 12, 2025, 03:22 UTC** — Chief Engineer reports to the bridge that
the No. 2 double-bottom tank has been breached and is flooding. Engine room
bilge alarms activate.

**October 12, 2025, 03:45 UTC** — Master declares an emergency. Vessel is
aground with a 3.5-degree port list. No immediate danger to crew. No
pollution observed.

**October 12, 2025, 04:00 UTC** — Master contacts Meridian Ship Management
(Cyprus) by satellite phone. Meridian's duty manager, Alexis Constantinou,
acknowledges and begins coordinating response.

**October 12, 2025, 06:30 UTC** — Meridian notifies Oceanic Syndicate 1847
(lead H&M underwriter) by email. Oceanic acknowledges at 07:15 UTC.

**October 12, 2025, 07:00 UTC** — Meridian notifies Harbour Mutual P&I by
email.

**October 12, 2025, 14:00 UTC** — Togolese maritime authority (Direction des
Affaires Maritimes) issues a directive requiring the vessel to engage a
local salvage master and submit a pollution prevention plan within 48 hours.

**October 13, 2025, 09:00 UTC** — Salvage tug Dorado arrives on scene.
Salvage contract executed on Lloyd's Open Form (LOF 2020), no-cure-no-pay
basis, between Master of Kestrel (on behalf of owners) and Dorado Salvage Ltd
(Abidjan).

**October 14, 2025, 16:00 UTC** — Kestrel is refloated on the high tide after
partial cargo discharge of approximately 12,000 MT of soybean meal into
barges. The vessel proceeds under own power to Lomé anchorage.

**October 15, 2025, 08:00 UTC** — Bureau Veritas class surveyor Capt.
François Delacroix inspects the vessel at Lomé anchorage. He imposes a
Condition of Class (CoC) requiring drydocking for underwater hull inspection
and repair of the No. 2 double-bottom tank before the vessel may proceed on
any further voyage.

**October 15, 2025, 08:00 UTC** — The Classification Clause in the H&M policy
is now triggered: a condition of class has been imposed. Cover is suspended
from this moment unless and until the condition is rectified and class
reinstated in good standing.

**October 15, 2025, 14:00 UTC** — Meridian notifies Oceanic Syndicate of the
Condition of Class by email. Oceanic acknowledges at 15:30 UTC.

**October 17, 2025, 10:00 UTC** — Oceanic Syndicate notifies Pinnacle Re
(Bermuda) of the claim. This is 4 days and 3.5 hours after Oceanic first
became aware of the grounding (October 12, 07:15 UTC). The 72-hour notice
provision in the reinsurance treaty has been breached.

**October 20, 2025** — Kestrel is towed to Tema, Ghana (nearest suitable
drydock). Drydocking commences.

**October 28, 2025** — Drydock repairs completed. BV surveyor Delacroix
re-inspects and lifts the Condition of Class. Class restored to good standing.

**October 28, 2025, 16:00 UTC** — Meridian requests written confirmation from
Oceanic Syndicate that H&M cover has resumed following reinstatement of class.

**November 3, 2025** — Oceanic Syndicate issues written confirmation that H&M
cover has resumed as of October 28, 2025, 16:00 UTC (the time of class
reinstatement, per the Classification Clause). Cover was suspended from
October 15, 08:00 UTC to October 28, 16:00 UTC — a period of 13 days and
8 hours.

**November 10, 2025** — Harbour Mutual P&I club notifies Kestrel Maritime
Holdings that it has received a cargo damage claim from Lagos Commodity
Trading Ltd, the cargo receiver, for USD 3,200,000 in respect of the 12,000
MT of soybean meal discharged into barges during the salvage operation. The
cargo receiver alleges that the discharged cargo was contaminated by seawater
and is a total loss.

**December 1, 2025** — Lloyd's salvage arbitrator is appointed. Dorado
Salvage Ltd files its claim under LOF 2020. Salvage security is posted by
Harbour Mutual P&I on behalf of Kestrel Maritime Holdings in the amount of
USD 4,500,000.

**January 15, 2026** — Bureau Veritas issues a class notation update: the
Kestrel's next intermediate survey is accelerated from September 2026 to
March 2026, citing the grounding history.

### SECTION D — The Coverage Dispute

#### D.1 — Oceanic's Position

Oceanic Syndicate accepts that the grounding is a covered peril under the H&M
policy (contact with a submerged object) but raises three defenses:

**Defense 1 — Classification Clause suspension:**
Cover was suspended from October 15 (CoC imposed) to October 28 (CoC lifted).
Any costs incurred during this period that are not directly attributable to
rectifying the condition of class are not covered. Oceanic contends that
the towing cost from Lomé to Tema (USD 85,000) and the drydock hire during
the period of suspended cover should be allocated between covered
(class-rectification) and uncovered (general maintenance) costs.

**Defense 2 — Navigation:**
Oceanic reserves the right to argue that the vessel's navigation contributed
to the loss, citing Ashworth's observation about the depth-contour margin.
Oceanic does not allege negligent navigation at this time but reserves the
defense. Under IHC 2003, the assured is not entitled to recover for a loss
caused by want of due diligence by the assured or the assured's managers. The
question is whether Meridian Ship Management's voyage planning constitutes
want of due diligence.

**Defense 3 — Late reinsurance notification:**
This defense does not affect the assured (Kestrel) directly. It is a matter
between Oceanic and Pinnacle Re. However, Oceanic has informed Kestrel that
any reduction in Oceanic's recovery from Pinnacle Re due to the late
notification may affect the speed of claim settlement. Oceanic acknowledges
this is not a ground for denying or reducing the assured's claim but states
it as a factor in timing.

#### D.2 — Kestrel's Position

Kestrel Maritime Holdings, through its solicitors Wavecrest LLP, responds:

**On Defense 1:** The Classification Clause suspends cover, but the costs of
rectifying the condition of class are themselves a covered claim under the
policy, because the condition arose from a covered peril (the grounding).
The towing to Tema was necessary to reach a drydock for class rectification
and is therefore part of the covered repair cost. Wavecrest cites *The
Bamburi* [1982] 1 Lloyd's Rep. 312 for the principle that costs reasonably
incurred in mitigating or repairing a covered loss are recoverable even if
incurred during a period of suspended cover.

**On Defense 2:** The vessel's navigation was consistent with available chart
data. The submerged object was uncharted. Even if the depth-contour margin
was narrow, the vessel was navigating in a recognized shipping lane with no
published hazard. Ashworth's observation does not establish want of due
diligence. Wavecrest cites *The Star Sea* [2001] UKHL 1 for the standard
of due diligence under marine insurance.

**On Defense 3:** This is acknowledged as a matter between Oceanic and
Pinnacle Re and does not affect Kestrel's claim.

#### D.3 — Harbour Mutual's Dual Position

Harbour Mutual occupies two positions simultaneously:

**As H&M following underwriter (25% line):** Harbour Mutual follows Oceanic's
lead on coverage decisions per the follow-the-leader clause in the slip, but
reserves the right to disagree on quantum. Harbour Mutual has not raised any
independent coverage defense.

**As P&I club:** Harbour Mutual has acknowledged the cargo damage claim from
Lagos Commodity Trading and is handling it under the club rules. The P&I
claim is separate from the H&M claim. The P&I deductible is USD 25,000
per incident. Harbour Mutual notes that if the cargo damage is found to
have resulted from unseaworthiness of the vessel, the club's right of
recovery against the member (Kestrel Maritime Holdings) may be affected.

#### E.5 — Loss of Hire Analysis

The Kestrel is on a time charter to Atlantic Grain Carriers SA at a daily
rate of USD 18,000. The charter party contains an off-hire clause that
suspends hire payments when the vessel is not available for the charterer's
use due to breakdown, damage, or drydocking.

The vessel was off-hire from October 12 (grounding) to October 28 (completion
of repairs) — a period of 16 days. However, 3 of those days (October 12–14)
were spent on salvage operations, during which the vessel was technically
still at the disposal of the salvage master under LOF, not the charterer.
The off-hire period attributable to repair is therefore 13 days (October 15–28
inclusive, adjusted for the CoC suspension period).

The H&M policy does not contain a Loss of Hire clause. Loss of hire is
typically covered under a separate Loss of Hire policy. Kestrel Maritime
Holdings does not have a separate LOH policy.

Wavecrest LLP argues that loss of hire should be recoverable as a
consequential loss of the covered peril. Oceanic's position is that
consequential losses are excluded under IHC 2003 Clause 23.4 absent a
specific LOH endorsement.

### SECTION H — Sanctions Compliance Check

During the voyage from Santos to Lagos, the Kestrel's AIS data shows a
brief stop on October 8, 2025 at Lomé anchorage (Togo). The stop lasted
approximately 4 hours. Meridian's records show this was for crew change
(one able seaman disembarked for medical evacuation; one replacement AB
embarked).

Togo is not a sanctioned jurisdiction under EU, US, or UN sanctions.

However, the vessel's original voyage instruction (issued September 28, 2025)
listed a port call at Lomé for crew change, and a subsequent port call at
Cotonou, Benin for bunkers. The Cotonou bunker call was canceled on
October 2, 2025 after Meridian determined that the bunker supplier had a
corporate parent entity on the US OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN)
list. Meridian's compliance officer, Dr. Hanae Yamamoto, recommended
cancellation and the call was not made.

The original voyage instruction naming Cotonou raises the question of whether
the mere issuance of a voyage instruction listing a sanctioned-entity port
call constitutes a breach of the trading warranty, even though the call was
canceled before it occurred. Under the Insurance Act 2015 (which applies to
this policy as a matter of English law), a breach of warranty that is
remedied before a loss occurs does not discharge the insurer if the breach
did not contribute to the loss. The Cotonou call was canceled 10 days before
the grounding. Oceanic has reviewed the AIS data, confirmed that the vessel
did not call at Cotonou, and has stated in correspondence (November 28,
2025) that it does not intend to raise the trading warranty defense.

Wavecrest LLP has responded (December 5, 2025) noting Oceanic's position and
stating that, even if the trading warranty issue were raised, the breach was
remedied before the loss and did not contribute to it, and therefore the
insurer would not be discharged under Section 10 of the Insurance Act 2015.

### SECTION I — Corrections and Clarifications

**Correction 1 (November 12, 2025):** Meridian's initial incident report
stated the grounding occurred at 03:22 UTC. The vessel's Voyage Data
Recorder (VDR) shows the impact at 03:17 UTC. The 03:22 time was the
Chief Engineer's first report of flooding, not the grounding itself. The
incident timeline is corrected accordingly.

**Correction 2 (November 25, 2025):** Ashworth's survey report initially
stated the No. 3 tank indentation was "approximately 15mm deep." Ashworth's
revised report (November 25) corrects this to "approximately 8mm deep,
within class society tolerance for shell plating deformation." The original
15mm figure was a preliminary field measurement that was superseded by
calibrated measurement during drydock.

**Correction 3 (December 20, 2025):** The salvage security amount was
initially reported as USD 5,000,000. The correct amount, per the LOF
arbitrator's direction, is USD 4,500,000. The USD 5,000,000 figure included
a contingency that was not ordered by the arbitrator.

**Clarification 1 (January 5, 2026):** Oceanic Syndicate clarifies that its
statement regarding the reinsurance notification delay (Defense 3) was
provided for transparency only and is not a defense to the assured's claim.
Oceanic confirms that the assured's claim will be adjusted on its merits
without reference to the reinsurance position.
