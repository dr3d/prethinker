# The Orchard at Severn Gate — Succession Record

## The Property

The orchard at Severn Gate comprises twelve named fruit trees on a half-acre plot in Lydbrook, Gloucestershire. The trees are: the King Pear, the Duchess Apple, the Cider Row (three trees counted as one unit), the Blenheim Orange, the Quince Pair (two trees counted as one unit), the Damson Wall, the Medlar, the Cherry Arch, the Worcester Pearmain, and the Greengage.

The property has been in the Pritchard family since 1948. This record reconstructs the current ownership, possession, and legal status of the orchard and each named tree based on family documents, solicitor correspondence, and a 2024 probate court filing.

## First Generation — Idris and Marged Pritchard

Idris Pritchard purchased the orchard in 1948. Upon his death in 1971, his will left the entire orchard to his wife Marged, with the instruction that upon her death it should pass equally to their two children, Gethin and Bronwen.

Marged died in 1989. Her will followed Idris's instruction and divided the orchard equally: Gethin received the western half (King Pear, Duchess Apple, Cider Row, Blenheim Orange, Quince Pair, Damson Wall) and Bronwen received the eastern half (Medlar, Cherry Arch, Worcester Pearmain, Greengage) plus a cash equalization payment of £600 because the eastern half contained fewer trees.

## Second Generation — Gethin and Bronwen

Gethin maintained his six trees and added a beekeeping operation in 1992. In 1997, Gethin took a £4,000 loan from a local agricultural cooperative and pledged the Cider Row as security. The pledge agreement states: "Ownership of the Cider Row trees remains with Gethin Pritchard. In the event of default, the cooperative may harvest the Cider Row for up to five seasons to recover the debt, after which the pledge expires."

Gethin repaid £2,800 of the loan before his death in 2004. The remaining £1,200 was unpaid. The cooperative exercised its harvest rights for the 2005, 2006, and 2007 seasons. Records show the cooperative harvested approximately £450 per season, totaling £1,350 — more than the outstanding debt. The cooperative acknowledged the debt as satisfied in a letter dated November 2007, but did not formally release the pledge in writing until March 2009.

Bronwen never married. She lent the Medlar tree to her neighbor, Douglas Pye, in 2001 under a verbal agreement: "You may harvest the Medlar each autumn for your preserves. This is a loan of harvest rights, not a transfer of ownership." Bronwen confirmed this arrangement in a 2003 letter to her solicitor.

Bronwen died in 2011. Her will left her four trees to her nephew, Gethin's son Rhodri. The will makes no mention of the Medlar loan to Pye.

## Third Generation — Rhodri Pritchard

Rhodri inherited all twelve trees: six from his father Gethin (including the now-satisfied Cider Row pledge) and four from his aunt Bronwen. He also received the £600 equalization payment obligation, which had never been paid to Bronwen and was absorbed into her estate.

In 2013, Rhodri's wife Carys asked Rhodri to give her the Greengage as a birthday gift. Rhodri wrote on a card: "The Greengage is yours, my darling. Happy birthday." No deed or formal transfer was executed. Carys has maintained and harvested the Greengage since 2013.

In 2016, Rhodri sold the Blenheim Orange to a cider company, Wye Valley Pressings, for £800. A proper bill of sale was executed. Wye Valley Pressings owns the tree but it remains physically in the orchard. They harvest it annually under an access agreement.

In 2019, Rhodri discovered that Douglas Pye was still harvesting the Medlar. Rhodri told Pye the arrangement with Bronwen had ended with her death. Pye disagreed, claiming Bronwen had told him the loan was "for as long as you want." Rhodri's solicitor advised that a verbal harvest loan from Bronwen, not being a formal easement, likely terminated on her death, but that Pye could argue adverse possession if he had harvested continuously and openly for twelve or more years. As of 2001 to 2019, Pye had harvested for eighteen years.

Rhodri did not pursue legal action. Pye continued to harvest the Medlar through 2024.

## The 2024 Probate Dispute

Rhodri died suddenly in September 2024. His will left the orchard "and all its trees" to his daughter Ffion. Carys, still living, challenged the will on the grounds that the Greengage was gifted to her in 2013 and should not be included in Rhodri's estate.

The probate court's interim ruling (January 2025) states:

1. The Greengage gift is disputed. A birthday card is not a deed. However, Carys has maintained the tree for eleven years. The court has not yet ruled on whether the card constitutes a valid gift or whether Carys's maintenance establishes an equitable claim. The matter is deferred to a full hearing.

2. The Blenheim Orange is not part of the estate. It was validly sold to Wye Valley Pressings in 2016.

3. The Cider Row pledge was satisfied in 2007. The formal release was executed in 2009. The Cider Row is part of the estate.

4. The Medlar situation is noted but not adjudicated. Pye has not filed a formal adverse possession claim. The court observes that Pye's continuous harvest since 2001 may support such a claim if filed, but no determination has been made.

5. The remaining trees — King Pear, Duchess Apple, Quince Pair, Damson Wall, Cherry Arch, and Worcester Pearmain — are uncontested and form part of Ffion's inheritance.

6. The £600 equalization payment from the 1989 division was never made. The court notes this as a historical debt of the estate but takes no action because the creditor (Bronwen's estate) has been fully distributed.

The probate matter remains open. Ffion holds provisional control of the orchard pending the Greengage hearing and any potential Medlar claim by Pye.
