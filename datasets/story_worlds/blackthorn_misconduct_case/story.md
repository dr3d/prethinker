# The Blackthorn Misconduct Case

## Source Document: Combined Investigation Record, Policy Extracts, Witness Statements, and Financial Trace

### SECTION A — Governing Policies

#### A.1 — Blackthorn University Research Integrity Policy (RIP-2024, Rev. 3)

All allegations of research misconduct must be reported to the Research
Integrity Officer (RIO) within 5 business days of the date the complainant
first becomes aware of the alleged misconduct. The RIO must acknowledge
receipt to the complainant within 2 business days of receiving the allegation.

Upon receiving an allegation, the RIO must determine within 10 business days
whether the allegation falls within the scope of research misconduct as
defined by this policy. Research misconduct is defined as fabrication,
falsification, or plagiarism in proposing, performing, or reviewing research,
or in reporting research results. Honest error and differences of opinion
do not constitute research misconduct.

If the allegation is within scope, the RIO must convene an Inquiry Committee
within 15 business days of the scope determination. The Inquiry Committee
must complete its work and deliver a written Inquiry Report within 60
calendar days of its first meeting, unless the RIO grants a written
extension. Extensions may not exceed 30 additional calendar days.

If the Inquiry Committee finds that a full investigation is warranted, the
RIO must notify the relevant federal funding agency within 30 calendar days
of the Inquiry Report's completion date. The RIO must also notify the
respondent (the person accused of misconduct) within 5 business days of the
Inquiry Committee's finding.

A formal Investigation Committee must then be convened within 30 calendar
days of the decision to investigate. The Investigation Committee must
complete its work within 120 calendar days of its first meeting, with one
permitted extension of up to 60 additional calendar days granted by the
Provost (not the RIO — the Provost alone may grant investigation extensions).

The Investigation Committee delivers a written Investigation Report to the
RIO. The respondent has 30 calendar days from receipt of the Investigation
Report to submit a written response. The RIO then has 10 business days after
the response period closes to forward the complete record (Investigation
Report, respondent's response if any, and all evidence) to the Provost.

The Provost must issue a final determination within 20 business days of
receiving the complete record. The Provost's determination is the
university's official finding unless the respondent appeals.

#### A.2 — Appeal Process (RIP-2024, Appendix C)

The respondent may appeal the Provost's determination within 15 business
days of receiving the determination by filing a written appeal with the
Faculty Senate Review Board (FSRB). The FSRB must convene within 20 business
days of receiving the appeal.

The FSRB reviews the complete record and may request additional testimony.
The FSRB must issue a written decision within 45 calendar days of its first
meeting. The FSRB may uphold, modify, or overturn the Provost's
determination. The FSRB's decision is final and not subject to further
university appeal.

If the FSRB overturns a finding of misconduct, the university must notify the
relevant federal funding agency within 10 business days of the FSRB decision
and must expunge the misconduct finding from the respondent's personnel file
within 5 business days.

If the FSRB upholds or modifies the finding, the sanctions recommended by the
Provost take effect immediately upon the FSRB decision, unless the FSRB
specifies otherwise.

#### A.3 — Federal Reporting Requirements (42 CFR Part 93, simplified)

When research funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF) or the National
Institutes of Health (NIH) is involved, the university must notify the
relevant agency's Office of Research Integrity (ORI for NIH) or Office of
Inspector General (OIG for NSF) at several points:

- within 30 days of deciding to open a formal investigation;
- immediately if at any point the university determines that public health or
  safety is at risk, that federal funds or equipment are threatened, or that
  the research record needs immediate correction;
- within 30 days of completing the investigation, with a copy of the
  Investigation Report.

The federal agency may conduct its own review and may accept, modify, or
reject the university's findings independently.

#### A.4 — Conflict of Interest in Investigation Proceedings

No member of an Inquiry or Investigation Committee may have a direct
professional or financial relationship with either the complainant or the
respondent. "Direct professional relationship" includes: current or recent
(within 3 years) co-authorship, shared grant funding, mentor-mentee
relationship, departmental supervisory authority, or membership on the same
active grant review panel.

If a conflict is discovered after a committee has been seated, the conflicted
member must recuse immediately. If the recusal reduces the committee below
its minimum size (3 for Inquiry, 5 for Investigation), the RIO must appoint
a replacement within 10 business days.

The RIO is also subject to conflict-of-interest requirements. If the RIO has
a disqualifying conflict with respect to an allegation, the Provost must
appoint an Acting RIO for the duration of the proceeding.

#### A.5 — Sequestration and Preservation of Evidence

Upon determining that an allegation is within scope, the RIO must immediately
sequester all research records, data, equipment, and materials relevant to
the allegation. "Immediately" means within 1 business day of the scope
determination, not within 1 business day of receiving the allegation.

The respondent and all persons who may have relevant evidence must be
notified of the sequestration order within 2 business days. Failure to
comply with a sequestration order is itself a policy violation subject to
separate disciplinary proceedings.

Sequestered evidence must remain in the custody of the RIO's office (not the
respondent's department) until the proceeding is fully resolved, including
any appeal.

### SECTION C — Incident Timeline

**January 8, 2026** — Dr. Samuel Achebe discovers apparent data irregularities
in Figure 3 of a paper published in the Journal of Catalytic Chemistry (JCC)
in September 2024, authored by Voss, Achebe, Petrova, and Schütz. The paper
reports results from NSF Grant CHE-2201847.

**January 10, 2026** — Achebe reports the allegation to RIO Chandrasekaran.
This is 2 business days after discovery, within the 5-business-day
requirement.

**January 13, 2026 (Monday)** — Chandrasekaran acknowledges receipt to Achebe
by email. This is 1 business day after receiving the allegation (received
Friday January 10, acknowledged Monday January 13), within the 2-business-day
requirement.

**January 24, 2026** — Chandrasekaran completes scope determination: the
allegation falls within the definition of research misconduct (specifically,
alleged falsification of spectroscopic data in Figure 3). This is 10 business
days after receipt (January 10 to January 24, counting only business days),
meeting the 10-business-day requirement exactly.

**January 24, 2026** — Chandrasekaran issues sequestration order for all data,
lab notebooks, electronic files, and equipment related to NSF Grant
CHE-2201847 in the Voss laboratory. Same day as scope determination —
within the 1-business-day requirement.

**January 27, 2026 (Monday)** — Voss, Achebe, Petrova, and Schütz notified of
sequestration order. This is 1 business day after the order (January 24 was
a Friday), within the 2-business-day requirement.

**February 10, 2026** — Inquiry Committee convened. Members: Prof. Kenji
Hayashi (Chair, Materials Science), Prof. Amara Diallo (Faculty Senate),
Dr. Rebecca Torres (External, MIT). This is 11 business days after the
scope determination on January 24, within the 15-business-day requirement.

**February 10, 2026** — Inquiry Committee holds first meeting.

**March 15, 2026** — Inquiry Committee requests 30-day extension from RIO
Chandrasekaran, citing difficulty obtaining Schütz's cooperation. This is
33 calendar days after the first meeting, within the 60-day initial period.
Chandrasekaran grants the extension in writing the same day.

**April 25, 2026** — Inquiry Committee delivers Inquiry Report. Finding: a
full investigation is warranted. The report notes that the raw spectroscopic
data files for Figure 3 appear to have been modified after the paper was
submitted to JCC, and that the modifications are inconsistent with standard
data processing. This is 74 calendar days after the first meeting (February
10 to April 25), within the extended 90-day deadline.

**April 28, 2026** — Chandrasekaran notifies Voss of the Inquiry Committee's
finding and the decision to proceed to investigation. This is 1 business day
after the Inquiry Report, within the 5-business-day requirement.

**April 29, 2026** — Chandrasekaran notifies NSF OIG of the decision to open a
formal investigation. This is 2 business days after the Inquiry Report,
within the 30-calendar-day federal notification requirement.

**May 20, 2026** — Investigation Committee convened. Members: Prof. Hayashi
(Chair, continuing from Inquiry), Prof. David Okonkwo (Faculty Senate),
Dr. Maria Santos (External, Stanford), Dr. Jean-Pierre Moreau (External,
CNRS Paris), Dr. Sarah Whitfield (Blackthorn, School of Medicine). This is
25 calendar days after the decision to investigate, within the 30-calendar-day
requirement.

**May 20, 2026** — Investigation Committee holds first meeting.

**June 2, 2026** — Prof. David Okonkwo discloses that he co-authored a review
article with Prof. Voss in 2024. This constitutes a co-authorship within 3
years and is a disqualifying conflict of interest per Section A.4. Okonkwo
recuses himself immediately.

**June 3, 2026** — Chandrasekaran appoints Prof. Ingrid Bergström (Blackthorn,
Department of Physics) as Okonkwo's replacement. This is 1 business day
after the recusal, within the 10-business-day requirement. The committee
remains at 5 members.

**July 15, 2026** — Prof. Schütz submits a written statement to the
Investigation Committee.

**August 1, 2026** — The Journal of Catalytic Chemistry formally retracts the
Voss et al. (2024) paper following its own editorial investigation. The
retraction notice cites "concerns about the integrity of spectroscopic data
in Figure 3."

**August 5, 2026** — NSF OIG contacts Chandrasekaran requesting an interim
report on the investigation status, citing the journal retraction as a
triggering event.

**September 10, 2026** — Investigation Committee delivers Investigation
Report to Chandrasekaran. Finding: research misconduct (falsification) by
Prof. Elena Voss. The report finds that Voss personally modified the
spectroscopic data files for Figure 3 after submission, that the modifications
materially changed the reported results, and that Voss did not disclose
the modifications to her co-authors. The report also finds that Dr. Petrova
was aware of the modifications but did not participate in them and did not
report them. This is 113 calendar days after the first meeting (May 20 to
September 10), within the 120-calendar-day deadline.

**September 10, 2026** — Chandrasekaran forwards the Investigation Report to
NSF OIG, within the 30-calendar-day federal requirement (same day as
completion).

**September 12, 2026** — Chandrasekaran sends the Investigation Report to Voss.
Voss has 30 calendar days (until October 12) to submit a written response.

**October 10, 2026** — Voss submits a written response denying the findings and
asserting that the data modifications were standard calibration adjustments
that she discussed with Petrova at the time.

**October 12, 2026** — Response period closes (30 calendar days from
September 12).

**October 23, 2026** — Chandrasekaran forwards the complete record
(Investigation Report, Voss's response, and all evidence) to Provost
Osei-Mensah. This is 9 business days after the response period closed
(October 12 to October 23, counting only business days), within the
10-business-day requirement.

**November 18, 2026** — Provost Osei-Mensah issues final determination:
misconduct finding upheld. Sanctions: 3-year suspension from serving as PI
on any externally funded research, mandatory research ethics retraining,
and a letter of reprimand in Voss's personnel file. This is 18 business
days after receiving the complete record (October 23 to November 18),
within the 20-business-day requirement.

**November 20, 2026** — Voss receives the Provost's determination.

**December 11, 2026** — Voss files a written appeal with the Faculty Senate
Review Board. This is 15 business days after receiving the determination
(November 20 to December 11), on the last day of the appeal window.

**December 22, 2026** — Faculty Senate Review Board convenes. This is 7
business days after receiving the appeal, within the 20-business-day
requirement. Members: Prof. Diallo (Chair), Prof. Larsson, Prof. Wu,
Prof. Bergström. (Okonkwo was originally a member but was replaced by
Bergström due to the conflict disclosed on June 2.)

**February 3, 2027** — FSRB issues written decision: the Provost's finding of
misconduct is upheld, but the FSRB modifies the sanctions. The suspension
from PI service is reduced from 3 years to 18 months, citing Voss's
otherwise clean record and the fact that one co-author (Petrova) was aware
of the modifications. The retraining requirement and letter of reprimand
are upheld. This is 43 calendar days after the first meeting (December 22
to February 3), within the 45-calendar-day requirement.

**February 3, 2027** — Modified sanctions take effect immediately per
Section A.2.

### SECTION D — Witness Statements (collected at various points during the investigation)

**Dr. Samuel Achebe (complainant, postdoc, Voss lab):**
"I noticed the discrepancy in Figure 3 while preparing a follow-up
experiment. The published spectra didn't match the raw data files I had
from when I ran the original experiments. I brought it to Dr. Voss first,
on January 6, and she told me it was a calibration difference and not to
worry about it. When I compared the file timestamps I saw the raw data
files had been modified after submission. That's when I went to Dr.
Chandrasekaran."

**Prof. Elena Voss (respondent):**
"The modifications to the data files were standard calibration adjustments
that I discussed with Dr. Petrova. Achebe was not involved in the data
processing for Figure 3 and does not have the expertise to evaluate whether
the adjustments were appropriate. I deny any falsification."

**Dr. Lina Petrova (research associate, Voss lab):**
"Профессор Восс действительно обсуждала со мной изменения в данных Figure
3. Она сказала, что это стандартная калибровка. Я не проверяла файлы сама.
Когда позже я увидела временные метки файлов, я поняла, что изменения были
сделаны после подачи статьи, но к тому моменту статья уже была опубликована.
Я не сообщила об этом, и я сожалею об этом."
[Translation: Professor Voss did indeed discuss the Figure 3 data changes
with me. She said it was standard calibration. I did not check the files
myself. When I later saw the file timestamps, I realized the changes were
made after the paper was submitted, but by then the paper had already been
published. I did not report it, and I regret that.]

**Prof. Martin Schütz (collaborator, co-author):**
"Ich war an der Datenanalyse für Figure 3 nicht beteiligt. Mein Beitrag zum
Paper beschränkte sich auf die theoretischen Berechnungen in Abschnitt 4.
Von den Änderungen an den Spektraldaten habe ich erst durch die
Untersuchungskommission erfahren."
[Translation: I was not involved in the data analysis for Figure 3. My
contribution to the paper was limited to the theoretical calculations in
Section 4. I only learned about the changes to the spectral data through
the Investigation Committee.]

**Dr. Priya Chandrasekaran (RIO):**
"I followed the timeline requirements as closely as possible. The one area
where I had concern was the Inquiry Committee's request for an extension —
Prof. Schütz was slow to respond to requests for documentation, which
delayed the inquiry. I granted the extension because I believed it was
justified. Regarding the sequestration, I want to note that when my office
took custody of the lab notebooks on January 27, we discovered that several
pages had been removed from Dr. Voss's primary lab notebook. The page
numbering jumps from 147 to 153. I reported this to the Inquiry Committee."

**Prof. Kenji Hayashi (Inquiry and Investigation Committee Chair):**
"両委員会の委員長として、私は手続きの公正さを確保するよう努めました。
調査中に最も困難だったのは、ペトロヴァ博士の証言でした。彼女はヴォス教授が
変更について話し合ったことを確認しましたが、自分でファイルを検証しなかったと
述べました。これは彼女を共犯者にするものではありませんが、報告義務に関する
問題を提起します。"
[Translation: As chair of both committees, I strove to ensure procedural
fairness. The most difficult aspect of the investigation was Dr. Petrova's
testimony. She confirmed that Professor Voss discussed the modifications
with her, but stated she did not verify the files herself. This does not
make her a co-conspirator, but it raises questions about reporting
obligations.]

**Prof. David Okonkwo (recused FSRB/Investigation Committee member):**
"I should have disclosed the co-authorship with Voss before agreeing to serve
on the Investigation Committee. The review article was published in Annual
Reviews of Catalysis in March 2024. I apologize for the oversight. I did not
believe it would affect my impartiality, but I understand the policy requires
recusal regardless of subjective belief."

**Equipment disposition:**
The Shimadzu UV-2600i spectrometer ($67,500) was purchased on Grant
CHE-2201847 in March 2024. Per university policy, equipment purchased on
federal grants with a unit cost exceeding $5,000 is the property of the
university but must be used primarily for the grant's research purposes
during the grant period. If the grant is terminated, the equipment reverts
to departmental inventory. Depreciation schedule: 7-year straight-line,
current book value approximately $48,214 (24 months of 84-month schedule
elapsed as of March 2026).

**Pending cost question:**
If NSF determines that the misconduct affected the validity of results
produced using Grant CHE-2201847, the university may be required to return
some or all expended funds. The university's General Counsel has advised
that personnel costs for individuals not involved in the misconduct (Achebe,
students) would likely not be subject to return, but that the spectrometer
and supplies directly supporting the falsified experiments could be at risk.
This is General Counsel's advisory opinion, not a determination.

### SECTION F — The Retracted Paper

Voss, E., Achebe, S., Petrova, L., & Schütz, M. (2024). "Enhanced catalytic
activity of nitrogen-doped carbon frameworks: A combined experimental and
theoretical study." *Journal of Catalytic Chemistry*, 312(4), 1847–1862.
DOI: 10.1016/j.jcc.2024.07.003.

- Submitted: March 15, 2024
- Accepted: June 28, 2024
- Published online: July 15, 2024
- Published in print: September 2024
- Retracted: August 1, 2026

The retraction notice, published by JCC, states: "This article has been
retracted at the request of the Editors following an investigation that
raised concerns about the integrity of spectroscopic data presented in
Figure 3. The authors were unable to provide the original unmodified data
files."

Note: The retraction was initiated by JCC's own editorial process, not
directly by Blackthorn University. Chandrasekaran's office was aware of
JCC's investigation but did not request the retraction.

### SECTION G — Achebe's Prior Informal Complaint (disclosed during investigation)

During the investigation, Achebe disclosed that he had previously raised
concerns about data handling practices in the Voss lab in an informal
conversation with Department Chair Yuki Tanaka in October 2025. Tanaka
told Achebe that she would "look into it" but took no documented action.
Tanaka has confirmed the conversation took place but states she understood
Achebe's concerns to be about general lab practices, not about a specific
paper or specific data.

This prior conversation is relevant because:
1. It raises the question of whether Tanaka had a reporting obligation under
   RIP-2024 (she did, if she understood or should have understood the
   concerns as an allegation of misconduct);
2. It affects the timeline: if the October 2025 conversation constituted
   notice, the 5-business-day reporting clock may have started then, not
   in January 2026;
3. Tanaka's account and Achebe's account of the conversation differ on the
   specificity of the concerns raised.

The Investigation Committee noted this issue in its report but did not make
a finding regarding Tanaka's obligations, stating that the question was
outside the scope of the Voss investigation and should be referred for
separate review.
