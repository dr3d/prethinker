# Greywell Pipeline Incident

## Combined Record: Operations Log, Witness Accounts, Draft Findings, and Status Notes

### OPERATIONS LOG (excerpts)

**March 2, 2026, 14:20** — Pressure alarm on Segment 4 of the Greywell
Industrial Water Pipeline. Alarm threshold: drop below 45 PSI sustained
for 3 minutes. Logged by SCADA operator Nina Ferris.

**March 2, 2026, 14:25** — Ferris dispatches field crew to Segment 4.
Crew lead: Rico Alvarez.

**March 2, 2026, 15:10** — Alvarez reports visual confirmation of a leak
at valve junction V-412. Estimated flow rate: 200 gallons per hour.
The valve appears to be partially unseated.

**March 2, 2026, 15:30** — Alvarez isolates Segment 4 by closing upstream
valve V-410 and downstream valve V-414. Leak flow stops.

**March 2, 2026, 16:00** — Shift supervisor Janet Yeong arrives on site.
Yeong orders a root-cause assessment.

### WITNESS ACCOUNTS

**Rico Alvarez (field crew lead):**
"When I got to V-412 the valve bonnet was loose — maybe two full turns
out from where it should be. It looked like somebody had been working on
it and didn't finish tightening. I checked the maintenance log on my
tablet and it showed no scheduled work on V-412 in the last 30 days."

**Nina Ferris (SCADA operator):**
"The pressure drop was sudden, not gradual. That's more consistent with
a mechanical failure or human intervention than with corrosion or wear.
But I'm not an engineer — that's just my observation from watching
pressure curves for eight years."

**Derek Hollis (night shift operator, March 1-2):**
"Ich habe in meiner Schicht keine Wartungsarbeiten an Segment 4
durchgeführt oder genehmigt. Ich habe gegen 02:00 Uhr am 2. März einen
Druckabfall von etwa 2 PSI auf Segment 4 bemerkt, habe ihn aber als
normale Schwankung abgetan, weil er sich innerhalb von 10 Minuten
stabilisiert hat."
[Translation: I did not perform or authorize any maintenance on Segment 4
during my shift. I noticed a pressure drop of about 2 PSI on Segment 4
around 02:00 on March 2, but dismissed it as a normal fluctuation because
it stabilized within 10 minutes.]

**Tomoko Saito (maintenance supervisor):**
"My team performed scheduled maintenance on V-410 — not V-412 — on
February 25. The work order is W-2026-0187. V-410 and V-412 are about
50 meters apart. It's possible someone on the crew accessed V-412 by
mistake, but I have no evidence of that. I'm not going to speculate."

**Unnamed source (via shift foreman's verbal report to Yeong):**
"Someone on the night crew mentioned they saw a contractor vehicle near
Segment 4 around midnight on March 1-2, but nobody got a plate number
or company name."

### DRAFT FINDINGS MEMO (March 8, 2026)

Prepared by: Shift Supervisor Janet Yeong
Distribution: Operations Director (for review, not for action)

"Based on my review of the available evidence, the most likely cause
of the V-412 leak was improper valve maintenance — specifically, the
valve bonnet was left partially unseated. The maintenance crew working
on V-410 on February 25 may have inadvertently accessed V-412.

I recommend that:
1. Maintenance procedures be reviewed to require valve ID verification.
2. The February 25 crew be interviewed.
3. SCADA alarm thresholds be tightened to flag 2 PSI drops as anomalies.

Note: this memo is my preliminary assessment for the Operations Director's
consideration. It is not a formal investigation report and should not be
treated as a finding or determination."

### STATUS NOTES

**March 10, 2026** — Operations Director Paul Marchetti acknowledges
Yeong's memo. He has not initiated a formal investigation. He asks
Yeong to interview the February 25 crew.

**March 15, 2026** — Yeong interviews the three members of the February 25
crew (Saito, J. Delgado, K. Osman). All three state they worked only
on V-410 and did not access V-412. None recall seeing a contractor
vehicle.

**March 18, 2026** — The valve bonnet at V-412 has been re-seated and
torqued to specification. Segment 4 is returned to service. Pressure
readings are normal.

**March 20, 2026** — Marchetti tells Yeong that he considers the matter
"operationally resolved" since the valve has been repaired and the
segment is back in service. He does not issue a formal finding on the
root cause. He does not adopt Yeong's preliminary assessment as an
official determination.

**April 2, 2026** — An anonymous tip to the Greywell compliance hotline
states: "The V-412 leak was caused by Derek Hollis. He was adjusting
the valve without authorization during his night shift." The tip
provides no supporting evidence.

**April 5, 2026** — Compliance officer Anika Prasad notes the tip and
forwards it to Marchetti. Marchetti responds: "There is no evidence
supporting this allegation. Hollis has denied any involvement. I am
not opening a formal investigation based on an unsupported anonymous
tip."

### CORRECTIONS

**Correction 1 (March 5, 2026):** Alvarez initially reported the leak
flow rate as 250 gallons per hour. After calibrated measurement by the
engineering team, the rate is corrected to 200 gallons per hour.

**Correction 2 (March 12, 2026):** The operations log initially recorded
the pressure alarm at 14:15. SCADA system logs confirm 14:20. Corrected.
