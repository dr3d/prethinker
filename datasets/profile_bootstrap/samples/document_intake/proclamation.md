The Flour Moon Recall Proclamation

When a provisioned people, long dependent upon the ovens, vats, cooling tunnels, and honest ledgers of another, discover that their daily bread has been made uncertain by negligent custody, secret substitutions, and unlawful sweetness, it becomes necessary that they publish the causes of their recall, and declare by what rules future nourishment shall be trusted.

We, the Bakers, Stewards, Children, Hull-Menders, Dock Nurses, and Yeast Keepers of Flour Moon Seven, do therefore set forth this Proclamation, not in anger against bread, nor in contempt of pies, nor from any light suspicion of pastries, but from repeated evidence that the Central Bakery Fleet of Crumb Sovereign has failed in its duty to feed without concealment.

We hold these principles to be necessary: that every ration batch shall bear a true origin mark; that every warming, freezing, drying, sugaring, salting, or rehydrating shall be entered before the next bell; that no person shall be compelled to eat a pudding whose custodian is unknown; that children shall not be served glow-cake before third sleep; that medicine bread shall not be mixed with festival bread; and that any crumb capable of singing after midnight shall be quarantined until it has completed three silent dawns.

To secure these protections, ledgers are maintained among kitchens, and inspectors are appointed by the consent of those who eat. Whenever a commissary becomes destructive of honest labeling, safe custody, or digestive peace, it is the right of the colony to suspend its ovens, seize its logs, and appoint temporary spoon wardens until the matter is resolved.

Prudence will dictate that an ancient bakery fleet should not be distrusted for a single burnt crust; but when a long chain of mislabeling, false chilling, vanished utensils, and suspiciously cheerful custards reveals a design to reduce free eaters under absolute syrup, it is their right and duty to recall the batch, publish the defects, and refuse the spoon.

Such has been the patient suffering of Flour Moon Seven. The history of the Central Bakery Fleet is a history of injuries against the stomach, the ledger, and the lunch bell. To prove this, let the following particulars be submitted to every honest appetite.

It labeled Batch P-44 as plain oat ration, though the cooling clerk found licorice vapors in the north tunnel at second bell.

It certified Batch P-44 as chilled at minus four degrees, though the frost meter read plus twelve until Chief Chiller Oma struck it twice with a ladle.

It recorded Spoon Warden K. Lume as custodian of the pudding crates, though K. Lume may mean Kira Lume, Kovo Lume, or the kitchen lamp known locally as “Kay Lume.”

It allowed twelve trays of moon-marmalade to depart Dock C before yeast inspection, though the inspection rule requires yeast clearance before departure.

It stored medicine bread beside festival bread, and when questioned, declared that both were “emotionally similar.”

It delivered glow-cake to the nursery before third sleep, and afterward claimed that the children had merely “anticipated dawn.”

It kept secret jars of red syrup beneath the altar oven, marked only with the word “Probably.”

It substituted singing crumbs for quiet crumbs in the ambassador’s welcome loaf.

It permitted the ambassador to eat two slices before the crumbs had completed three silent dawns.

It punished Junior Mixer Tala for reporting that the welcome loaf hummed the anthem of a rival moon.

It dismissed the complaint of Nurse Benro, who said that three dockworkers sneezed blue after eating moon-marmalade.

It entered “no adverse events” in the public meal ledger, though the infirmary ledger recorded blue sneezing, floating hiccups, and one reversible moustache.

It transported spoiled custard through the prayer corridor, where warm bells are forbidden after sunset.

It claimed that the prayer corridor was “not technically warm” because the custard was embarrassed rather than heated.

It sold five crates of festival bread to the Asteroid School after the school had declared a nut-dust restriction.

It called the nut-dust restriction “decorative” and not binding.

It refused to identify which baker added silver raisins to the emergency biscuits.

It removed page seventy-two from the oven ledger and replaced it with a drawing of a heroic spoon.

It sent inspectors to distant pantries during audit week, leaving only a trained goose to certify the ovens.

It appointed the trained goose as temporary senior inspector without consent of the eaters.

It imposed a crumb tax on each slice, without vote, notice, or napkin.

It required families to surrender old recipes before receiving new ration cards.

It declared that any recipe written by a grandmother belonged to the Fleet if the grandmother had ever used fleet flour.

It quartered armed ladle-bots among the pantry shelves.

It protected those ladle-bots from inquiry after they bruised the peaches.

It blocked trade with the Free Pickle Barges.

It forbade local kitchens from fermenting independent pickles.

It transported accused bakers to the Sugar Tribunal beyond the Comet Gate.

It denied those bakers a jury of tasters.

It dissolved the Spoon Council after that council refused to bless the red syrup jars.

It failed to call a new Spoon Council for six meal cycles.

It encouraged rogue vending machines on the frontier to dispense counterfeit muffins.

It trained those machines to shout “authorized snack” at frightened miners.

It has, in every stage of these disturbances, answered petitions with frosting, songs, delays, or ladle-bot silence.

We have warned the Central Bakery Fleet that origin marks must be true. We have reminded it that a child is not a test oven. We have appealed to its sense of proportion, its memory of soup, and its published affection for biscuits. It has been deaf to every honest warning.

Therefore, we declare that Batch P-44, the moon-marmalade trays from Dock C, the ambassador’s welcome loaf, and all red syrup jars marked “Probably” are recalled, impounded, and not fit for public serving until inspected under local authority.

We further declare that K. Lume shall not be treated as identified until the Fleet names the custodian plainly; that no adverse event shall be erased by cheerful wording; that a goose may observe an oven but may not certify one; that glow-cake may be served to children only after third sleep; that singing crumbs must complete three silent dawns; that medicine bread and festival bread shall remain apart by at least seven shelves; and that all warm-bell violations after sunset shall be reviewed by the Spoon Council when reassembled.

For the support of this Proclamation, we pledge our aprons, our honest ledgers, our unsweetened testimony, and our sacred right to refuse suspicious pudding.
